The Ontological Argument

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Of those 6 options, only 3 of them seem actively viable. P1 seems like a logical truth. (Assuming we’ve done our possible-being metaphysics correctly.) Surely our three categories of being really do constitute a legitimate partition. Those 3 options really do seem to be both exhaustive and mutually exclusive. P3 & P5 are both definitions. It typically doesn’t make sense to say that a definition is false, unless the definition is reporting on the world – unless the definition has an empirical truth value. The def in P3 is a logical entailment. An analytic truth that falls out of the way we’ve described Category 2. Nothing to argue with there. What about P5? Maybe we can argue with Anselm’s def of God? Probably not. In general I think, here and in other cases, it just doesn’t make a ton of sense to argue with a definition.

SLIDE 21
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
The Greatest Possible Island Reply – Is the GPI really a coherent, imaginable being?

[PRESENTER NOTES]
… The GPI, unlike Anselm’s God, is fundamentally a finite being. It is, at the end of the day, just an island! We might wonder, Does it even make sense to talk about an island as being perfect ? It seems coherent on its face, but can we actually imagine such a place? How many squares miles is it? It has to have a size, and that size has to be perfect! So… what’s the size? How many different kinds of fruit does it have? It has to have some number of fruit-bearing species, and that number has to be the perfect one! So… what’s the number? Likewise: what is the sand like? What is the weather like? What is the animal life like? What kinds of natural ores are there? The point is this: As soon as one ties to imagine the specific perfections of the GPI, one is confronted with the fact that it’s not-at-all clear how to make sense of the perfect island size or the perfect number of fruit-bearing species . So this is one thing Anselm might say in response to Gaunilo: God, unlike the island, can actually be coherently imagined, in detail. We can say with some specificity what properties God has. Thus, while the GPI is probably in Cat 3 (an impossible being), God is not.

SLIDE 33
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Principle – adding a new property to a concept results in a new concept

[PRESENTER NOTES]
This example is meant to illustrate a general principle that Kant seems to accept: Namely, when you add a new property to an existing concept, the result is a new concept. (thus, when you add the property one-horned to the concept horse, the result is the new concept unicorn .)


SLIDE 34
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Kant reasons, If existence is a property , then an existing unicorn is not a unicorn at all. ( Unicorn + new property = new concept ) Obvs, an existing unicorn is a unicorn . Thus, existence is NOT a property .

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Why isn’t an existing unicorn a unicorn? Remember our general principal: WHen you take a concept and add a new property, the result is a new concept. So IF existence is a property, adding the property to the concept unicorn results in a brand-new concept. (C.F. adding the new property one-horned to the concept, horse, results in a new concept.) But surely an existing unicorn really is a unicorn. (that is to say, surely we don’t think that the concept of a unicorn that exists is genuinely distinct from our concept of a unicorn – not in the way that our concept horse is genuinely distinct from our concept, unicorn. Obviously, Kant doesn’t actually mention unicorns. But this seems to be the essential pattern of his objection. We must deny that existence is a property because were it a property, our concepts of existing things (like existing horses or unicorns or whatever) would be clearly distinct from our concepts of those same things, sans existence. But that’s stupid. An existing horse just is a horse. That’s the same concept. Thus, existence is not a property.

SLIDE 44
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
P1) God is either a Category 1 , a Category 2 , or a Category 3 entity. P2) God is not logically impossible (is not Category 3 ) P3) By definition, God is not Category 2 C) So, God is in Category 1 , is real The Ontological Argument (un-sneaky’d)

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Not a good argument! No one should accept P2, given the definition in P3. What the R.L. case teaches us is that just because we can imagine something doesn’t mean that thing is possible. We seem to be able to imagine Real Leprechauns, even though they are impossible. (b/c they don’t actually exist and by definition aren’t merely possible.) Likewise, just because we seem to be able to imagine God as the greatest possible being, that doesn’t mean God – as Anselm defines Her – isn’t impossible.
