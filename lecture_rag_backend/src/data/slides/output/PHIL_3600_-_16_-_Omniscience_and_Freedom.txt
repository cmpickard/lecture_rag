God is omniscient OR human choices are sometimes free Pick One.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
To be clear, the point is that these two ideas appear to be in conflict. The argument isn’t that humans aren’t free, full stop. The argument is that IF God is omniscient, THEN humans cannot be free. ALTERNATIVELY… IF humans are free, then God cannot be omniscient Even better, we should say: the point of the argument is that we have to choose between human freedom and God’s omniscience. Those two things are incompatible, so we have pick one and leave the other: We can say either: God is omniscient. Since she already foreknows what we’ll do, it’s not in our power to do otherwise; our choices are never free. OR we can say: human choices are often genuinely free. God Herself does not know what we’ll do until we do it, meaning God is not omniscient. She lacks knowledge of the future.

The first was actually already articulated in this quote from Augustine. He was trying to explain why it matters that divine omniscience and human freedom are incompatible... His worry is that we can’t make sense of sin, can’t hold people responsible for their own misbehaviors, unless people sin freely and voluntarily.


SLIDE 37
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Our legal system and everyday moral practices assume that people deserve certain kinds of treatment, good or ill, on the basis of their behavior. But if we never act freely , then that attitude is incoherent at best. Put differently, how can we hold people responsible for actions over which they had literally zero control ?

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Put differently, … The thought is: If God infallibly foreknew all of the bad things I’d ever do, then none of my choices were really UP TO ME, in the right sort of way. Before I was born, God already knew cheat on my taxes or whatever. it was already true that i’d do that. But, then, surely I can’t be held responsible for a bad deed that was casually fixed before I was even born. That’s sounds like crazy talk! Compare: suppose I make an assassination robot from scratch. I program it to pick up a gun, walk next door, and kill my next door neighbor – scripting out all of those actions in detail and in advance. Such that: I foreknow exactly what my robot will do when I turn it on. Surely, the robot itself is not responsible for killing my neighbor. And that should be true, regardless of how cognitively sophisticated my robot is. Like, suppose I give it conscious experiences and a rich intelligence. It is AWARE of itself as a being as it marches to my neighbors house and shoot him. Still: my robot is not responsible for that killing. How could it be? Given its programming, it doesn’t have any control of that act. It couldn’t do otherwise. This is, essentially, the worry that Augustine is raising about God’s omniscience. If God foreknows everything we’ll do, then we are all like conscious little robots, following the hard-coded scripts that God has foreordained for us. And, if that’s right, no one is ever morally responsible for anything. No one ever deserves to be punished (or rewarded) – by us or by God. Because no one ever really chooses anything, neither righteous nor wicked.

A 3-O God never has to choose between preserving human freedom and preventing evil because… there is no human freedom to preserve.

Objections

[PRESENTER NOTES]
So, the problem is that it looks like the theist can’t have both divine omniscience AND human freedom . That leaves the theist with 3 options; (1) Accept that God is omniscient; deny that humans make free choices (2) Accept that humans make free choices; deny that God is omniscient (3) Find some way to argue that both can be true: God is omniscient and humans are free OBvs, most theists want to go for that last option. So I want to finish our discussion by looking at some objections from theists, some attempts to thread the needle and argue that Omniscience and Freedom are compatible.


SLIDE 40
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Objection We’ve gotten the explanation backwards, God’s beliefs don’t force us to act ; our free actions force God to believe .

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Recall, the concern is that you choose to buy the Handitaur BECAUSE God always and already knew that you would. First, God knows, THEN that knowledge determines and explains why you act the way that you. This objection reverses that explanation: First, you act the way that you do (you buy the Handitaur), THEN that action determines and explains why God believes what she does (i.e. believes that you choose to buy the handitaur)


SLIDE 41
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Does Obama exist because you know that Obama exists? Or do you know that Obama exists because he does ?

[PRESENTER NOTES]
The theist might point out: this is normally how beliefs work anyway! Compare: ask yourself... The world isn’t Peter Pan , and Obama isn’t Tinkerbell. We didn’t and can’t bring Obama into the world just by believing in him hard enough. Rather, first he existed and that caused us to have the belief, Obama exists. Point being: beliefs are fundamentally RESPONSES to the world. First, something is true in the world, THEN that causes us to adopt a certain belief (the belief that the thing is true.) The reverse sounds very silly: it’s not as though first we believe something and THEN our believe causes that thing to be true. The theist’s point is: surely God’s beliefs are like that too! First, the thing happens, then God believes it, in response.

----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Reply First, the claim was never that God’s knowledge forces us to act; it merely signals that we’re unable to do otherwise. Second, this objection requires backwards causation. (Which is probably incoherent!) Objection We’ve gotten the explanation backwards, God’s beliefs don’t force us to act ; our free actions force God to believe .

[PRESENTER NOTES]
… It’s not like God’s knowledge is some golem standing behind us holding our arms and making us do stuff. That’s was never the claim. Rather, the claim is that we can tell that our actions aren’t free from the fact that God infallibly foreknows what we’ll do. (COMPARE: you can tell that someone is asleep from the fact that they’re snoring – snoring signals sleep. But that doesn’t mean that snores cause people to sleep . ) Worse though, is the fact that this objection commits us to some pretty dubious metaphysics.

SLIDE 48
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Objection God’s foreknowledge is like that of a friend who knows you really well. (i.e. accurate predictions don’t make you unfree !) Reply Your friend’s foreknowledge isn’t really like God’s b/c your BFF is fallible. (It is in your power to surprise your friend!)

[PRESENTER NOTES]
… Your friend, while very accurate, can still be wrong. It is very much in your power to surprise your friend, to make her wrong. You could set out to be, like, so random one day, and if you tried hard enough and introduced some genuine uncertainty into your decision-making (like rolling some dice or something), you would successfully break your friend’s foreknowledge. She can’t predict that you’ll roll a 6 or whatever. She’s not Omniscient, just very observant In short, the problem is that this objection seems to misunderstand the problem. The problem isn’t that God expects us to do certain things and is right a lot. The problem is that God is ALWAYS and INFALLIBLY RIGHT. So much so that, it’s impossible for us to surprise God, to break her omniscience. It’s God’s infallibility which undermines our freedom, not the fact that She knows your pretty well.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Zooming back out, recall the big picture… As always, there’s more to be said here! There are other possible replies open to the theist –- maybe she can argue that we’re understanding freedom wrong and that the true view is quite compatible with omniscience?
