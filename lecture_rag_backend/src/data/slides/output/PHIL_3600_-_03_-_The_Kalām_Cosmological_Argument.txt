Why does the universe exist at all – why isn’t there nothing? Option 1 – the universe has always existed (nothing was never a possibility) Option 2 – the universe just started existing one day (and for no reason) Option 3 – the universe started to exist for a reason (call it ‘God’)

[PRESENTER NOTES]
The genetic question for the Cosmological Argument – you will not be surprised to learn – concerns the existence of the cosmos, the universe. The question is… (… surely it was possible that nothing existed rather than the universe. So why didn’t that happen? What’s the explanation?) The proponent of the Cosmological Argument distinguishes 3 possible answers to this question: First, we might think that the question doesn’t make sense b/c the universe is infinitely old. If so, if the universe has always existed, then the question doesn’t arise. (Why isn’t there nothing? There has never been nothing – nothing has never been an option. There has always been the universe.) That’s Option 1. But maybe you don’t think that’s right. Maybe you think the universe did begin to exist at some point. If so, our question does make sense: Why did that happen?! Why did the universe suddenly start existing when before it didn’t? There seem to be, basically two answers. Either: Literally no reason. The universe just started existing one day, uncaused, unintended, uncreated. That’s Option 2. Or: For a reason. The universe started existing one day because something caused it to exist – a something that we might as well call ‘God’. That’s Option 3.

Cosmological Arguments have a rich history in the West, going back at least to Plato. The basic idea is, Given the way the universe is, we can infer that there must have been a first cause. These arguments aim to rule out Option 2. (“the universe just started one day for no reason”)

[SLIDE BODY]
The Kalām version of the Cosmological Argument was developed by the Medieval Islamic scholar Al-Ghazali. This version tries to show that the universe began to exist – thus, ruling out Option 1 (“the universe has always existed”)

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Whereas as most Cosmological Arguments are concerned with ruling out Option 2 – where the universe just exists for no reason – Al-Ghazali acknowledged that Option 1 was also on the table and so he developed a more sophisticated version of the Cosmological Argument which seeks to demonstrate that the universe cannot be infinitely old. His argument works, in effect, by trying to show that it’s impossible for anything to be infinitely old – or infinite in any respect.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
ANother bit of scientific evidence for P1 is this: The second law of thermodynamics states that the total entropy of a closed system always increases over time. The universe is a closed system, so the entropy of the universe is ever-increasing. ( entropy can be a tough concept to wrap one’s head around – maybe think of it as a measure of the uniformity of temperature in a volume. That’s not right, but it’s close enough for our purposes.) This is significant for 2 reasons: First, Since entropy is currently non-zero, we should expect it to approach zero as we look further back in time. (remember, entropy is always increasing – so running time backwards should cause it to decrease.) Since negative entropy is impossible, we’ll eventually hit a point in time when the entropy of the universe is zero. That’s the Big Bang – that’s when the universe started.

Second, the fact that entropy is increasing in the universe means that end state for our universe is something called ‘heat death’ – a situation in which all matter and energy is smeared out uniformly across the universe. (i.e. a universe in heat death doesn’t have any matter clumps like planets or humans or any energy clumps like stars.) If it helps, you can think of the Big Bang as a bit like permanently throwing open the doors of a refrigerator. At first there’s a big temp diff between the inside of the fridge and the surrounding room, but after enough time passes, that difference is gone. It’s all the same temp. (The Big Bang is actually the opposite – it was a concentrated HOT thing that cooled as it expanded out, rather than a concentrated COLD place that was invaded by warm air. But – still – it’s a workable for our purposes.) Quiz Question: Can you see why these two facts imply that the universe had a beginning? (Fact #1: entropy in the universe is always increasing. SLIDE 24

[SLIDE BODY]
There are different sizes of infinity . (called ‘cardinalities’) The Natural Numbers and The Integers are both the smallest size . The Real Numbers are a larger size of infinity . There is no maximum size. The number of cardinalities is itself infinite .

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Here’s an important fact that you probably didn’t learn in high school. There are different sizes of infinity (called cardinalities ). And those two sets we were just looking at are both the smallest size. (And, hence, the same size! Which is surprising!) Also, the real numbers are of a larger size. [though, recent proofs suggest, not ONE size larger.] The real numbers, you may recall, are all the numbers that express a position on a continuous number line. (Which includes fractions and irrational numbers like PI.) T his fact is a relatively new discovery - it wasn’t proven until ~140 years ago by Georg Cantor. It’s quite a bit beyond our scope to look at Cantor’s proof, but it’s actually not all that complicated, as mathematical proofs go. You can find some good Youtube videos explaining it, if you’re curious. And there’s a Mathematical Logic course here in the CU Philosophy Dept that covers it, too, if you want a full philosophical treatment. (Quickly, though, the gist of the proof is this: two infinities count as being the same size if and only if there’s a bijection function relating them – where a bijection is a one-to-one mapping of items in one set to items in the other). Cantor demonstrates that the real number cannot be bijected with any countably infinite set, like the natural numbers. Thus, the real numbers have a greater cardinality than the natural numbers.) But again – that’s beyond our scope, so no worries if that went over your head.) As a final important fact, it turns out that there is no maximum size of infinity. We’ll see in a sec why it matters that infinities come in different sizes.
