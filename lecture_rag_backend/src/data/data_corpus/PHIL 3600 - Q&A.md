

## On the weirdness of the ontological argument arg
**Lecture:** The Ontological Argument

The argument's method is \-- and I cannot stress this enough \-- genuinely, bafflingly strange.

Anselm asks us to think really hard about the concept of God. By doing this, he claims, we will simply see (using pure logic\!) that our concept of God applies to something that really, actually exists in the world.

But.... WHAT?\!

That just seems so obviously confused as a methodology. Surely you can't figure out which things exist in the world just by sitting around with your eyes closed and thinking really hard?\! ("Are there any dodo birds left in the world?" is not a question you can answer by just thinking really hard on it.)

Of course, that doesn't tell us why or where the argument goes wrong. But it does seem to make the argument unpersuasive. It's too weird to work\!

## On the assumption that every being belongs in exactly 1 category
**Lecture:** The Ontological Argument

Good question. Let me take a crack at answering it.

There's a background assumption at play here which is that *every being is in exactly one of our three categories. **Every** being: from bananas to pokemon to round squares \- each particular kind of being is either Cat 1 (and NOT in 2 or 3\) or in Cat 2 (and NOT in 1 or 3\) or in Cat 3 (and NOT in 1 or 2).* This assumption hopefully seems pretty intuitive\! A being that exists (i.e. is in Cat 1\) can't *also* be a being that doesn't exist; a being that is impossible (i.e. is in Cat 3\) can't *also* be a being that's possible. 

Thus, Anselm's God also belongs in exactly one of the categories. That's why we can't place God in *both* 1 and 2\. No beings are in two categories at once.

## On conceivability and possibility
**Lecture:** The Ontological Argument

When we try to actually sort various beings into our 3-Categories, we ultimately confront the question, *How do we tell whether a being is impossible or not? What distinguishes the impossible beings from all the rest?*

In lecture, I relied pretty heavily on the metric of *conceivability.* If you can **conceive** of a being, probably that means it's possible. And if you can't even conceive of it, probably that means it's impossible. But, as you point out, *conceivability* is an imperfect proxy for possibility\! Some of the things in the universe may be *inconceivable by human minds* and yet still exist. (e.g. the dimensions posited by string theory) And yet other things may *seem conceivable* while actually being impossible. 

One famous example: *the barber who shaves everyone who does not shave themselves:*

Suppose there's a town, and in that town is a barber. The barber has a policy: for any man in town, the barber shaves that man if and only if that man does not shave himself (like, at home or whatever.) But wait: the barber lives in town, so does the barber shave himself? Well, if the barber DOESN'T shave himself, then, by his own policy, he must shave himself. (He shaves all the men who don't shave themselves\!) And, on the other hand, if he DOES shave himself, then by his own policy, he doesn't shave himself. Either way, we've run ourselves into a logical contradiction: the man both does and does not shave himself. The description of our barber appears incoherent.

But then... if we can't rely on our ability to **conceive,** how are supposed to tell which beings are impossible? And, moreover, doesn't that undermine P2 of the Ontological Argument? ("God is not in Cat 1 b/c we can imagine God.") Apparently, the fact that we can imagine something may not actually tell us all that much about whether that sort of being can exist\!

## On how Rowe is using the word ‘impossible’
**Lecture:** Objections to the Ontological Argument

 Part of what's going on is that there are these concepts of *possibility* and *necessity* and *impossibility* that are already present in our everyday language. Like, normal people really do say things like, "It's impossible to go faster than the speed of light" and "it's impossible to be angry at you for more than a few minutes" or "It's impossible to multiply a number by two and have the result be odd."

Pretty clearly the notion of *impossibility* being used in those three sentences is very different\! What Rowe is trying to do is take one of those meanings (the third one) and try to be a bit more precise about what is being claimed. He's suggesting: *when we say it's impossible to multiply by two and get an add number, we're saying something not just about OUR abilities but about what can be done **anywhere** by **anyone.** Noone can multiply by two and get an odd number\!*

That suggests that it might be useful to think about *other hypothetical universes* when we consider which thinks are impossible. Like, imagine a universe with really different physical laws than ours. Can the people in *that* universe divide by two and get an odd number? Seemingly not\! If that's right, then it seems like this rule of ours ("It's impossible to divide by two and get an odd number") really is true **everywhere \--** and not just, like, around here.

## Is Anselm allowed to make up his own def of God?
**Lecture:** The Ontological Argument

I think that last point your raise is a particularly interesting an important concern about the Ontological Argument. To many people, it feels a bit illicit for Anselm to introduce his own definition of 'God' and then argue that *that* being exists. Like, what about people who don't think of God as *the greatest possible being*? Isn't there room for reasonable disagreement about what God is like?

One thing we can say in Anselm's defense is this,

*Strictly speaking,* the Ontological Argument doesn't ever need to use the word 'God' to work. Anselm could have used another name instead: **Let's define 'George' as the greatest possible being.** In that case, what his argument would show is that George exists. Really, then, the implication of the Ontological Argument is that there is in existence in our universe an entity who meets the description 'the greatest possible being'. Anselm wants to call that entity 'God', but you don't have to, if you don't want\! He's content if you accept that George exists, where 'George' is the name of the greatest possible being.

## Another explanation of contingency, necessity, and possibility 
**Lecture:** The Ontological Argument

One helpful way of thinking about these concepts (*necessity, impossibility, possibility)* is to imagine an all-powerful God creating a bunch of different universes and filling them with different things. Not for any particular reason, just playing around with it. Using this metaphor, we can describe beings as being *necessary* or *contingent* or *impossible* according to whether God has a choice in placing them in each universe.

Most beings are **contingent** \-- God **can decide** whether to include them in each universe. Centaurs? Maybe\! Some universes may include them and others don't. ATMs? Maybe\! Some universes include them and others don't. (Some of the contingent beings are **real,** meaning they actually exist in our universe. Others are **not-real but possible.** They don't actually exist in our universe, but an all-powerful God *could have* included them no problem.)

Some beings are **impossible** \-- God **never** gets to include them in his universes. (examples might include a round square. Round squares don't make sense, are incoherent, so God can't create them and can't insert them into any universes.)

Finally, some beings are **necessary** \-- God **always** has to include them in each universe. (examples might include **oneness** \-- i.e. the number one, not the arabic numeral '1'. Can God create a universe and not have the number one be there? Surely not\! After all, the universe itself is **one thing.**)

So in lecture and in the reading, we were just asking of different things, "Which category does this belong in? Is this something that God **can't** create? Or does God get to choose?" 

Will Smith and Will Smith's evil twin are both contingent. Our hypothetical God can decide whether to include them or not. Dogs-that-are-taller-than-themselves are impossible. Our hypothetical God never gets to include those b/c they are incoherent.

## The ketchup case is a category-mistake
**Lecture:** The Ontological Argument

We'll probably need to refine our definition of 'Great-making Property' to exclude the possibility for category mistakes \-- which, I think, is what's going on in the cases you're pointing to.  
A **category mistake** occurs when we incoherently attribute a property to a being which can't have properties of that sort. A classic example:  
   
*I am giving you a guided tour of CU's campus.*   
*"Look, that's the library\!" I say. "And there's the Philosophy building and that over there is the campus gym."*  
*"But..." you ask, "where on campus is the school spirit? You haven't pointed it out yet."*  
   
We call that a **category mistake** b/c *CU's school spirit* isn't the sort thing that has a physical location. It's not the right *category* of being; it's confused to ask after the physical address of an abstract intangible like school spirit.  
So probably we'll want to reformulate our definition of 'great-making property' to make it clear that category mistakes don't count as counter-examples. Maybe: *a great-making-property is one such that, invariably, adding the property to a being which lacks involves either a category mistake or makes that being greater.*  
Then when some asks about kind ketchup, we will simply say: That's a category mistake\! Ketchup isn't the sort of thing that can be kind or mean, so we don't learn anything about the great-making nature of *kindness* from talking about kind ketchup. (Likewise if someone asks about *strong* ketchup or *happy* ketchup.)

## **‘Better’ in what sense?** 
**Lecture:** The Ontological Argument

For *existence* to be a great-making quality, it really must be true that every single case we can think of where we add *existence* to a being which lacks it results in a *better* (or *greater*) being. And the cases you're pointing to suggest maybe that's not true.

Your objection leads me to wonder,

*How exactly should we be understanding betterness in this context?* Like, better in what sense? Morally better? More impressive? More praiseworthy? Better at survival/fitness (i.e. evolutionarily better)?

Those are genuinely different metrics\! Adding a property to a being might make it better in one of those senses and not in another. (e.g. a being that's *extremely altruistic* might be morally better but evolutionarily worse.) It seems like we need to be able to pin down what exactly 'better' is supposed to mean before we can properly assess which properties count as *great-making*.

The fact that Anselm ultimately wants to say that *God has all the great-making properties* does help us somewhat, I think. We can rule-out, e.g., the *evolutionarily better* interpretation since Anselm surely isn't trying to say that God is the being with maximal fitness. But I'm less certain about the other options. If a great-making property is one that makes beings morally better, then Mecha-Hitler cases (super-evil entities who exist only in our imaginations) would seem to show that existence is not a great-making property. On the other hand, if a great-making property is one that makes a being more impressive, it's less clear that Mecha Hitler cases count as counter-examples. (Though, now we probably need to try to be more specific about what 'impressive' means. \*sigh\*)

## On saying “God exists for you but not for me”
**Lecture:** The Kalām Cosmological Argument

Interesting questions\! I'll take my best shot at explaining what I \*think\* Craig takes himself to be doing. (and how other philosophers interpret what Craig is doing\!)

Mostly, when philosophers say that something "exists", they mean that it's **real** or **actual** or **a part of reality.** Indeed, one of the sub-areas of philosophy, Metaphysics, just is the study of what exists. That is to say, Metaphysics is all about figuring out which things *exist* or *are real*.

As an analogy, imagine a small basket sitting in my closet and full of my stuff. We could make a list of all the things in the basket (maybe, e.g, a hairbrush, a couple of pennies, a baseball, a dead cricket, a lockbox, and 3 textbooks.) Maybe we work really hard to make sure our list is perfectly accurate and complete: everything in the basket is on the list and there is nothing on the list that isn't in the basket. Now let's suppose that the lockbox in the basket is sealed shut and you and I disagree about what's inside. You think it's a bunch of Pokemon cards while *I* think it's a bunch of old medical documents. Which is to say: we disagree about what belongs on the List of Things in the Basket. *You* think the list should include Pokemon cards; *I* think it should include Old Documents.

In this case, I think, it clearly doesn't make much sense for us to say something like, "Maybe we each get our own list? Maybe what's in the basket differs from person to person?" That just sounds confused. After all, the *whole point of the list* is **be accurate,** to **correctly describe the contents of the basket.** It's true that we *don't know* what actually belongs on the list (Pokemon cards or old documents), but there's still a fact of the matter. Either there are Pokemon cards in there or there aren't. If so, 'Pokemon cards' belongs on the list. If not, they don't. It doesn't really make sense to say that the Pokemon cards "exist-for-you-in-your-basket" but "don't-exist-for-me-in-my-basket." **There's only one basket\!** We are looking at the same, single basket, and we are disagreeing about what's inside.

 

Now imagine a similar list for the universe, a list of all the things that are in the universe: I'm on the list because I'm in the universe. You're on the list because you are too. Grizzly bears, coffee cups, anchors, and icebergs are all on the list. On the other hand, unicorns, time machines, earth's 2nd moon, and Sherlock Holmes are all **not** on the list. Those things aren't actually in the universe.

When Craig says "God exists", what he means is, *God is on the list of things in the universe.* And when an atheist says, "There is no God," what she means is, *God is **not** on the list of things in the universe.* And as in the basket case, the background assumption is **there is only one universe.** Craig and the atheist are looking at the same, single universe, and they are disagreeing about whether God is a part of it.

Thus, Craig takes himself to be giving us reasons for believing that God really does belong on the List of All Things in the Universe. (Translating the argument into our list metaphor, it says something like: "Every list needs a list-writer\! Thus, the List of All Things must include the list-writer Herself\!") 

As for people who refuse to believe in the existence of things that others take to be obvious (e.g. a man who insists that "Whales aren't real, bro\!"), I think it makes more sense to say that those people are *ignoring* the proof (or, better, ignoring the *evidence*) rather than to say that *there is no proof.* 

(In general, I think that talk about "proof" is kinda confusing for complicated linguistic reasons. I think it's clearer to talk about "evidence." We can say, e.g., that there is *decisive evidence* that whales are real. And yet, there could still be people who ignore that evidence \-- people to whom the existence of whales is "unproven." Those people are just wrong about the existence of whales, though. We shouldn't think that their wrongheaded skepticism somehow undermines the evidence for whales\!)

Does that help at all? Was that even an answer to anything that you were asking?

## Is nothingness not itself a something?

But what about absences? Like: do shadows belong on the LRT? holes? silences? darkness? pure void? (maybe some of those things but not all? If so, which things and why?) Your point, I take it, is that **IF** we think that absences are real things, then Craig's principle is vacuous. It came from this bit of void here \-- and, keep in mind, voids are *bona fide* parts of reality\!") I suspect Craig is not going to be moved by this objection b/c, I'd hazard, he would not think that absences are real; he would not include them on the LRT next to dogs and quarks.

## What about giving a material interpretation to “nothing comes from nothing”?
**Lecture:** The Kalām Cosmological Argument

 think there's some ambiguity in Craig's principle that *something can never come to be from nothing.* He might be thinking that's true in the sense that everything comes to be *for a reason* **OR** he might be thinking that that's true in the sense that everything comes to be *out of pre-existing material.* (Or, Craig might be thinking that both are true?)

If I'm understanding you correctly, it sounds like you want to press on that second interpretation?

The material interpretation of the claim *does* seem very plausible\! 

A wooden statue can never come from **brand new** matter; it must come from some pre-existing stuff \-- viz. the matter of a tree. And of course the tree's matter comes from pre-existing matter too, all the matter that a tree takes-in to run its metabolism. (e.g. water, CO2, nitrogen from the soil.) And *those* things used to be other things, which used to be yet other things. And so on, back through time. The same is true of every other material object: when it comes into existence, all of its matter / energy is derived from other things that existed before.

You seem to be asking: *What about the universe? Where did all the matter in the universe come from?*

That's a good question\!

I think Craig is going have to say either **(a)** from God. Or **(b)** from nothing.

On (a), the view is that God literally took a part of Herself and used it to generate all the matter of the universe (and since matter/energy are convertible, all the energy of the universe too.) On (b), the view is that God has the power to create matter/energy from nothing at all. That is to say, on (b), Craig is denying the principle that *all material things come from other material things.* God, at least, is able to violate that principle and create matter *ex nihilo.*

Now, you might think both of those options are going to run Craig into trouble.

Suppose Craig answers your question about the material source of the universe by saying (a). "All of the matter/energy that was used to create our universe pre-existed the universe. That matter/energy used to be part of God."

If that's the story, then it seems we should ask the same question about God. *Where did God's material come from?* By claiming that the universe's matter used to be God's matter, Craig would seem to be endorsing the principle that *all material things come from other material things \--* not even the universe is an exception\! But then... whence God's matter? Has it just... always been? If so, are to we to assume that God has diminished Herself by making the universe from Her material substance? If not \-- if God is just as large post-Universe-Creation as before but also God really did remove some of Herself to make the universe \-- then that entails that God is actually infinite in material scope. (Only with a thing of infinite size can you substract a large amount and end up with the thing being the same size as before.) But, we're told by Craig, actually infinite things are impossible. This is troubling. So either Craig accepts that God is made of matter/energy **and** God diminished Herself when She made the universe OR Craig goes with (b).

But (b) (i.e. the creation of matter *ex nihilo*) seems to create other problems\! Craig is a big fan of treating natural regularities as exception-less rules, and here's a natural regularity that we've only ever seen obeyed and never violated: *matter/energy is always conserved.* Since that's always been true in the past, then by Craig's reasoning, that's surely true of God and the universe, too.  So... (b) doesn't work either. The universe must also have been created from conserved matter/energy.

Thoughts about any of that? (you or anyone else\!)

## Hume teaches us that there is no causation. Checkmate Craig.
**Lecture:** Objections to the Kalām Cosmological Argument

Right, so, Hume was famously skeptical of causation. His view was that we can't know whether our commonsense notion of \***causation**\*, as a relationship that obtains between events whenever one event precedes, explains, mechanistically gives rise to the other, corresponds to any real relationship that actually exists in the universe. (i.e. he was skeptical about whether causation is a real thing.) And he was skeptical for the reasons you've outlined: our *evidence* that causation is real is just our observations that certain events consistently happen before/after other certain events. From those correlations, we've *inferred* that causation is a real thing. But... we could be wrong. All our evidence is equally compatible with causation **not** being real. It could be, instead, that the universe is no more than one thing happening after another.

Your objection, I take it:  
If we can't *know* that causation is real, then *a fortiori* we can't know that everything that begins to exist has a cause. So we can't know whether P1 is true. (Notice: that doesn't mean P1 is *false*\! Rather, the Humean about causation would withhold judgment about the truth of P1 \-- and, thus, about the success of the C.A.)

As it happens, [not that many philosophers are Humeans about causation](https://survey2020.philpeople.org/survey/results/4998)

## On what we can infer about God
**Lecture:** The Fine-tuning Argument

You might be right that we can infer more things about God than I suggested in lecture. (about, e.g., Her power and knowledge) To create the universe, She certainly needed \*lots\* of power and \*lots\* of knowledge. That much seems exactly right.

However, I'm not sure it follows that she needed \*all of\* the power and knowledge. Suppose, e.g., that at the dawn of time, God didn't have the power to play guitar and didn't know how many jelly beans can fit into a soup can. Surely humans are capable of engaging in this same sort of reasoning about all sorts of things they might encounter in the world. We can infer that a building must have had a builder because given some concrete and rebar subject to random forces, the macrostate pile of rubble is realized by billions upon billions of microstates while the macrostate upright building is realized by a relative few. Therefore, etc.

## On defining omnipotence and omnibenevolence
**Lecture:** The Problem of Evil

We define 'omnipotence' as 'the capacity to bring about any logically possible state of affairs' in order to preclude certain (stupid) objections to the 3-O God.

## Previewing the Soul-making Theodicy
**Lecture:** The Soul-making Theodicy

Yeah, nice\!

This spin on the Free Will Theodicy is starting to sound a lot like the Soul-making Theodicy. (Which we'll talk about in the next module.) It's a pretty plausible theodicy, all things considered\! 

The basic thought is this: take any evil you like, it will be true that God could stop that evil. Of course She could. She's Omnipotent and Omniscient. But that would be like God doing our math homework for us. Which is to say: it would rather defeat the point\! God *could* do our math homework; God is very good at maths. But the point of math *homework* is for us to do it ourselves, for us to *learn math* by doing it. My math homework is a necessary evil *that I have to deal with myself.* It is necessary for *me to struggle with my homework myself* in order to achieve the greater good of learning math. No struggle; no learning.

Similarly: God could stop evils; she is very good at doing that. But the whole point of there being evil at all is for us to *deal with it ourselves.* We should be the ones to stop evils b/c we are the ones who desperately need to mature and grow wise and compassionate. We should be the ones to make our world a better place because we, and not God, need the practice. Thus, the greater good that God secures by allowing suffering to occur is the maturation of us humans. No struggle; no maturation. And, on this view, the end goal of all this suffering is for all humans to be brought, in time, into communion with the 3-O God. But only once they've had a chance to mature. (to *make our souls better*, hence the name: the Soul-making Theodicy)

One important thing to notice about this theodicy, in contrast to the Free Will Theodicy is this:

on the Soul-making Theodicy, suffering is justified because it **promotes personal growth,** which is a very good thing. That is, on the Soul-making Theodicy **suffering *causes* good things** to happen. On the Free Will Theodicy, in contrast, suffering is the *effect* of good things \-- or, put differently, **good things *cause* suffering.** (Free choice is good, but free choices cause suffering. Since the goodness of freedom outweighs the badness of suffering, it's better to preserve freedom than to prevent suffering.)

Accordingly, I think your suggestion is akin to the Soul-making Theodicy b/c you're telling the same sort of story. You are explaining how **suffering might cause good things.** (which is backwards from the story we get from the FWT\!)

(ii) What about sociopaths – i.e. people who appear incapable of moral maturation? Why does the 3-O God create people like that?

## On the decision procedure for a 3-O God minimizing suffering
**Lecture:** The Problem of Evil

Good questions\! I'll take a shot at answering them, as best I can, on behalf of the atheist.  
Since the 3-O God is by definition Omniscient, She presumably has knowledge about the future. She knows, for instance, what will happen if she prevents a particular car crash, and she knows what will happen if she doesn't prevent that car crash. Presumably, the 3-O God will decide what to do using the following procedure:  
(1) She will notice a bad thing that's going to happen soon \-- like a car crash.  
(2) She will look into the future to assess all of the consequences of this car crash. That is, how much good will done by this crash? How much bad? What is the net-value of this event, from now until the end of time?  
(3) She will look into the future to assess all of the consequences of Her stopping the car crash. How much good will be done? How much bad? What is the net-value of stopping the crash, from now until the end of time?  
(4) She will select the option with the highest net-value. (If stopping the crash results in a better world, She'll do that. If allowing the crash results in a better world, She'll do that.)  
Something like this procedure has to be the one that the 3-O God will follow. Her Omniscience lets her figure out which events are necessary for maximizing the good in the world, her Omnipotence lets her bring about those maximally-good events, and her Benevolence means She always wants to bring about the best possible world.  
That's why you will sometimes hear the Problem of Evil stated this way,  
P1) If the 3-O God exists, then ours is the best of all possible worlds.   
P2) Our world is not the best of all possible world.  
C) The 3-O God does not exist.  
And the atheist will support P2 by pointing out all the ways that our world could be better. (e.g. it could contain fewer genocides, it might have not included Alzheimer's Disease, it could contain less child abuse and domestic violence, etc.)

I test you on course materials to find out what you've learned. If I had perfect knowledge of the future, I wouldn't need to give tests. Surely the same applies to God.  
   
One reason this question is so important is that it presents the theist with a dilemma,  
Option \#1: The 3-O God knows the future.  
This is troubling for the reasons you point out. AND, it also seems to create problems for our understanding of free will. If God has knowledge of the future, then She already knew before you were even born ever single thing that you would ever choose to do. For any particular decision you make (e.g. the decision to eat toast for breakfast rather than eggs), God has known since the dawn of time that you'd do that. But then... is there any meaningful sense in which your decisions are ever free? Like, you chose toast for breakfast, just as God has always known you would. Could you have chosen eggs? Were you free to choose eggs? Seemingly not. For, choosing eggs would surprise God, would break Her Omniscience. And surely it's not in your power to break God's Omniscience.  
Thus, if God knows the future, we should be concerned that human choices are never truly free.  
Option \#2:  The 3-O God does not know the future  
This is troubling b/c it makes the Problem of Evil way worse. Like, recall the God's Plan Theodicy. The theist says, Every bad thing that's ever happened has happened according to God's morally perfect plan. God lets natural disasters, wars, and genocides happen because each of those things is necessary for making our world the best-possible-place. That's not a very satisfying Theodicy, but that story does have to be true, if the 3-O God exists. If there's a 3-O God, then everything that happens is in accordance with her morally perfect will.  
Only, if God doesn't know the future, then that story is probably false. God's Perfect Plan only makes sense if God has knowledge of the future; you need to know the future in order to perfectly plan for it. Like, consider a genocide. God sees the genocide about to happen and decides not to stop it. Why? Well, not because She knows that the future goes best if the genocide happens. Because God doesn't know the future\! Likewise for many other instances of suffering. If God doesn't know the future, then God doesn't know which instances of suffering are in-the-long-run necessary and which are in-the-long-run unnecessary. Which is a big problem\!  
   
Someone who believes in the 3-O God seemingly must choose one or the other of these options, but it looks like both have very serious problems\!  
Do you (or anyone else\!) have any thoughts about which Option is more plausible / a better choice for the theist?

Etc.

## Maybe the alien would choose the religion that maximizes the chance of salvation? 
**Lecture:** The Problem of Religious Pluralism

That's interesting \-- and probably right, from a game-theoretic perspective. 

Consider this toy example,

There are 3 world religions (A, B, and C) that offer salvation. But they differ in how widely they offer it. Religion A says that only A-believers are saved. Religion B says that both A and B believers are saved. And Religion C says that A and B and C believers are saved. All agree that non-believers go un-saved, and all 3 religions can freely be joined by anyone. Which religion should you join, if you want to maximize your chances of salvation?

 

If your only goal is to maximize your chances of salvation, the answer is clear: *you should join Religion A.* That's because everyone agrees that A-believers are saved\! (Whereas C-believers only get saved if Religion C is true. Bad odds; don't pick that one\!)

That's just a toy example, but the upshot is clear enough: *if your goal is maximize your chances of salvation, you should join whichever religion has the highest number of votes as a "legitimate path to salvation."*

 

That's kinda strange... what are we to make of that? 

Well, we might note, first, that that implication only arises for people whose only decision criteria is *trying to maximize their own chance of salvation.* That's probably no one \-- or next to one. At least, I've never met anyone w/ that sort of baldly pragmatic approach to religious belief. And, second, we might point out that that's not how beliefs work most of the time. Just because a certain belief would *benefit me* doesn't mean I can adopt that belief at will. If, e.g., there were some evidence that Flat Earthers tend to be much happier than Round Earthers, I doubt I would be able to simply *will myself* into earnestly believing that the earth is flat \-- even though that would be good for me. Similarly, then, even if I can see that Religion X is the one that's most likely to lead to salvation, I might not be able to actually adopt all the beliefs that are required for counting as an X-er. 

 

All of this is skirting pretty close to Pascal's Wager. He famously argued that we should believe in God for pragmatic reasons: if we believe, we either go to heaven or waste a bit of our time. But if we don't believe, then we either go to hell or save a bit of time. Given the stakes, then, it makes far more sense to believe. 

That argument is complicated by the existence of multiple religions, and for exactly the reasons you pointed out\! (The different religions have complex beliefs about which other religions get saved\!)

## Why do so many religions agree AT ALL about the big picture stuff?

Theists, obviously, want to say that it's because **God is real**.  (Which, yeah \- maybe\!)

 

But what I actually find more interesting (as you seem to\!) are the alternative explanations for those religious commonalities. Here are a couple different ones:

(1) Evolution. Early hominids who believed in a transcendental reality had a fitness advantage over those who don't. Thus, we evolved to be open to the sorts of ideas that most religions share.

(2) Social cohesion. In ancient civilizations, shared belief in a transcendental reality helped hold society together. Cultures w/o these believes quickly fell apart.

 

Both of those explanations seem to raise a bunch of further questions. Like, *what exactly is the fitness advantage enjoyed by religious humans?* And, *Why couldn't the social-cohesion benefit be achieved by other sorts of social arrangements? What's so special about religious beliefs in particular?* 

Here are a couple other explanations one might give for religious commonalities:

(3) Viral Ideas. There used to be lots of other religions that *didn't* believe in salvation (e.g. ancient Greek religion), but those religions didn't spread as well as the salvific religions. People are more likely to convert to a new religion when it has something rather than nothing to offer (i.e. salvation.) Nowaday, the un-viral religions are mostly gone, having been outcompeted in the marketplace of ideas.

(4) Intolerance and Conquest. Most of the current world religions emphasize the importance of spreading the religion to others. Historically, not all religions were like that. (again, think about the Greeks and Romans.) Through a long history of conquest and intolerance, the religions that emphasize growth have dominated and stamped out the passive, tolerant, non-salvific religions.

## What counts as “sufficient”?
**Lecture:** The Ethics of Belief

Yeah, good\!

I think that is THE big question for Clifford's principle. He says we may only believe on the basis of "sufficient" evidence, but we're not told what counts as sufficient\! What standards are we to use? How much evidence must we gather? Surely we aren't supposed to dedicate like, *literally our whole lives,* to the gathering of evidence just to avoid the immorality of having ungrounded beliefs\!

In some sense, that question *(What counts as* *sufficient*?\!) is way, way, way too big for Clifford to tackle in a short essay. One could write multiple books trying to answer that question. (People *have* written multiple books\!) But the point remains: we need to know what counts as sufficient evidence if we're to know what we're morally allowed to believe or know which people we can condemn as believing wrongly\! Should we be condemning children for believing in the tooth fairy? Should we be condemning our ancestors for believing in lightening gods?

 

This is, like, a classic hard problem in philosophy. We have an important idea where we can all agree about the extremes, the easy cases. (e.g. We all seem to agree that you *do* have sufficient evidence for believing in whales. We all seem to agree that the shipowner did **not** have sufficient evidence for believing in the soundness of his ship and that he is culpable for that.) And then there are a bunch of hard cases in the middle where we aren't sure what to think. (e.g. Are children required to meet the same standards? How are we supposed to tell which facts count as junk facts?)

In cases like these, usually philosophers will try to do some theorizing to generate some plausible account that gets all the easy cases right and has plausible, helpful things to say about the hard cases. I share your intuition that Clifford's principle is too strong, but there's also something right about it, it seems\! So... we need some weaker, more nuanced account of sufficient evidence.

We can already roughly see what that account must look like, I think. We probably will want to count testimony (i.e. the things that other people say or report) as sufficient in a lot of cases. Otherwise, it's immoral to have beliefs about history or about any science you aren't an expert. (Like, what evidence do you personally have that Genghis Khan existed? Only the say-so of teachers and the like. But... surely it's not immoral for you to believe in Genghis Khan. So probably *the say-so of other people* should count as sufficient evidence.) We'll also want to think about the quality / importance of sensory evidence. (*When should we trust our senses and believe thing on the basis of our sensory evidence? When **shouldn't** we trust our senses? etc.)*

 

So... yeah. It's a huge, complicated topic. You are in good company in being stumped here\!

## Quick Def of evidence
**Lecture:** The Ethics of Belief

"Evidence" is being used here to mean something like, "an observation that seems to count in favor of (or justify) a particular belief." Public evidence consists in observations that we can share (e.g. photos, videos, arguments.) Private evidence consists in observations that we can't share. (e.g. the feeling of my head aching \-- i can \*tell\* you about it, but i can't share the feeling.)

Why should we think there's such a thing as private evidence? The basic thought is: sometimes we really do experience things that genuinely seem to justify our having certain beliefs (e.g. an experience of seeing an angel descend from the heavens to speak to me, a headache), even though we cannot share those experiences with others. The fact that we cannot share those observations doesn't seem to render them *evidentially worthless*, in other words.

(C) Therefore, the atheist can believe in universal moral laws, no problem.

(1') Clearly, there **can't** be moral laws without a lawmaker. That's nonsensible. Atheism is incompatible with a belief in objective, universal moral laws.

(2) **Analogy Claim**: scientific laws are basically the same as ethical laws

##   **Who enforces morality on DCT?**
**Lecture:** Divine Command Theory

If by "enforce" you mean, *makes sure that rule-breakers receive a just punishment,* then the answer is probably *God.* God is the enforcer of Her own moral rules. She's the one who metes out punishment and reward for moral and immoral behavior.

But you mustn't, and you'll be punished if you do. That's what moral laws are like. There's a moral rule against murdering. But you *can* murder. That's a physical possibility. Except you mustn't, and you'll be punished (by God \-- and also by the U.S. govt) if you do.

We look back at, e.g., Thomas Jefferson and can easily point out all of his moral shortcomings and problematic beliefs. P1) If the Mental Content view is correct, then a person, X, who completely loses their memories is **not identical** to the earlier persons w/ the same continuous body as X

P2) But X **IS** identical those earlier people (despite not remembering them)

C) The Mental Content view is not correct

What the Mental Content proponent says here is going to depend on whether they think **memory** is the only important thing for personhood. If they *do* think that memory is all that matters, I suspect they'll bite the bullet. ("I accept the implication. X is not the same person as those earlier people w/ whose bodies X is continuous.") As you point out though, most people aren't going to be inclined to agree.

But a lot of Mental Content View fans **dont** think memory is all that matters. They think that sameness of personality is also part of what matters. (and other things too: sameness of values, dispositions, core beliefs.) If **that's** what matters for personhood, then we should reject P1. It simply isn't true that that version of the Mental Content view implies that X is strictly non-identical to those earlier people. It's not like each date finds her with a radically different personality\!)

 

Here's a complication, though: while the Memories Only view gives us clean answers about which people are identical ("X and Y are the same person just in case one of them remembers being the other."), the Memory/Personality/Values/Etc. view doesn't always give us clean answers. 

Consider a person, P, who suffers a very serious blow to the head, one that significantly alters her personality/values etc. *Is post-injury P the same person as pre-injury-P?* Suppose post-P is \~20% different from pre-P. Maybe in that case we'll say that post-P and pre-P are the same person. But what if the difference is 21%? 22%? 23%? 30%? 40%? 60%?

At some point, it's going to obvs that post-P and pre-P are different people. (e.g. when they're 80% different.) But where does the change happen? Is there some precise value at which post-P and pre-P cease to count as the same person? Surely not\! Surely that's absurd\! (We have to say things like, "If one more neuron had been damaged, P would have died. But, thankfully, that didn't happen. So P is exactly the same person as before." ... That sounds *very implausible* to me. How could **one neuron** among billions make the difference between complete survival and complete destruction?\!)

This is a common objection to the Memory/Personality/Values/etc. view. *Unlike* the Memories Only view, it doesn't give us clean answers about who's identical to whom in these partial-change cases. (And, insofar as we require the view to give us answers, it has to draw an arbitrary line: *any tiny change beyond this point and the two people are not the same.*)

## On parfit’s ‘No Further Fact’ view
**Lecture:** Personal Identity & the Afterlife

If we had more time, I'd have you all read a famous article by the philosopher Derek Parfit who responds to this objection by embracing what he calls a "No Further Fact" View about personal identity. Parfit accepts the Mental Content view of Personal ID, but he also argues that it's a mistake to insist that there must always been a clean Yes/No answer to questions about whether 2 people are literally the same person. (i.e. a mistake to insist that there will always be **some further fact.**) That's a bit telescopic, so let me give you a different case where it's easy to make sense of a No Further Fact View.

Imagine that you and I are having a bit of small talk, and you ask me where I'm from. I say, "It's complicated\! I was born in Oklahoma, but i was only there for 8 months. Then I spent 2 years in NC. Then 7 years in KS. Then 5 years in ND. Then 4 years in NE. Then 1 year in MT. Then 7 years in CO. Then 2 years in MO." 

You say, "Oh, interesting\! That's a lot of places\! But which of those are you **from,** though?"

I say, "I don't think it's like that. I was in OK for a little bit. Then NC for a bit. Then KS, ND, NE, MT, CO, and MO. That's where I've been; that's where I'm from."

You say, "Nonono, you're not understanding me. Like, which particular place is *The Place That You are From."*

I say, "I.... don't think that question has an answer? **There's No Further Fact** of the matter about where I'm from over-and-above the list I just gave you. The list is all there is."

In this case, I have a No Further Fact View about *Where I'm from.* I can list off where I've been, but, i think, it's a mistake to insist that there is some further fact, over-and-above the list, about where exactly I'm from.

That's what Parfit thinks about personal ID. Suppose we describe another blow-to-the-head case where the person experiences really significant changes in their mental content. (e.g. they lost 67% of their memories, have a personality that's 35% different, have 28% different values, etc.) If we ask Parfit whether post-P is the same person as pre-P, he'll say, "Well, they lost 67% of their memories, 35% of their personality, 28% of their values..." That's it; he'll give us that list but won't say *Yes* or *No.*

And if we *insist* ("I know all that\! But **are they the same person?**"), Parfit is going to shrug and say the same thing, "Well, they lost..." He doesn't think the *Yes* or *No* question has an answer.

On his view, it's a mistake to insist that there always must be some further fact of the matter about whether two people existing at different times count as *the same.* (in the same way that it's a mistake to insist that there must always be some further fact about where exactly each person is from.)

Any thoughts / objections / questions about that dialectic? (you or anyone else\!)

## When might a death count as good-for-you?
**Lecture:** The Badness of Death

Hmm, yeah interesting. *When, if ever, would death count as a good thing \-- and why?*

Philosophers who work in conception ethics often make use of the notion of *a life that's better than no life at all.* It's commonly thought, for instance, that conceiving a child is morally permissible but only if you expect that that child's life will be better than no life at all. If not \-- if, say, you know that your as-yet-unconceived child will be born with Tay-Sachs \-- then it's immoral to conceive that child. (the thought being: Tay-Sachs is so bad that it's better never to have been than to exist with Tay-Sachs. So you wrong the child by bringing it into the world with a life that's worse than no life at all.)

Similar remarks would seem to apply (as you point out) to lives that already exist. Sometimes it may turn out that a person's future is so bad, that it's better for them to die than to keep living. In those cases, death would seem to be a benefit rather than a harm.

I think that's plausible \-- i.e. that it's coherent to think that that could happen \-- but it's not entirely uncontroversial. (e.g. some religious traditions hold that in every moment of life, your *being alive* is really, really good for you. So much so that no one is ever *better-off-dead,* no matter how wretched their circumstances.)

Your suggestions about *meaningfulness* also strike me as plausible. Maybe the fact *that we'll die* is important for our lives having meaning. Perhaps our choices about how to spend out time *matter* precisely because we have only a finite amount of time to spend. And if that weren't true, if we were immortal, maybe our choices wouldn't have much weight. An immortal person can usually choose A **and** B \-- maybe A right now, but can circle back to B later, in the fullness of time.

That might be right\! Still, we should want to distinguish a couple different versions of this idea:

(i) The fact *that you'll die as some time or other* is important for giving your life meaning

(ii) The fact *that you die when you do* is important for giving your life meaning.

That first claim seems plausible but the second doesn't. (at least, not to me.) Like, imagine a kid who dies in a car accident at age 17\. It seems wrong to say, *If she hadn't died at 17 \-- if she'd lived to 80 \-- her life wouldn't have been meaningful.* 

That suggests that (a) *dying sooner rather than later is a misfortune* (in most cases) but (b) *dying at some point is a benefit.* Immortality is bad for us, but realllllllllly long lives are good for us.

Does that seem right to you? Thoughts about that? (you or anyone else\!)

## How do we explain the badness of murder, if death isn’t bad for us?
**Lecture:** The Badness of Death

People who agree with the Epicureans may be able to make sense of the wrongness of murder in most cases, but their explanations will be **very weird.** They might say, for instance,

While death itself is not a harm, there are many other bad things that come alongside a typical murder. Being murdered usually *feels really bad.* It hurts; it's very scary. Those are bad things\! Hurting someone is a harm; so is making them feel very afraid. Also, murder victims leave behind family and friends. All of those people feel terrible about their friend's having been murdered. All of those bad feelings are harms.

What makes murder wrong, in most cases, is the fact that the murderer wrongfully imposes all of those harms on the victim and on their loved ones.

That said, we must acknowledge that those harms aren't always present. If you kill me painlessly and without scaring me and if I don't have any friends or family, then you haven't done any harm. No harm; no foul. That particular kind of killing isn't wrong.

 

That's... not the most intuitive and plausible explanation for the wrongness of killing. (surely the primary wrongness of killing is the fact that it ends the life of the victim\!) But my point is that the atheist Epicurean does have *some* ability to make sense of killing being immoral.

You're right, though, that this is a very unappealing consequence of the Epicurean view\! The Epicurean has mostly implausible things to say about the intuitive wrongness of killing.

I think you're right that this is a good reason *not* to agree with the Epicureans about death.

## What about suicide? Is suicide rational, on the Epicurean account? 
**Lecture:** The Badness of Death

Yeah, good\! I think these questions about *suicide* are a natural next-step, after considering the badness of death in general.

A couple thoughts:

(i)

One thing that *super-*complicates any discussion of the rationality of suicide is mental illness.

Like, compare the decision-making of an ideal rational agent with the decision-making of a person with severe (but temporary) depression. An ideal rational agent, by definition, would be making a sober assessment of the expected value of their future. (e.g. *There's a 10% chance that the rest of my life will contain more good than bad; a 25% chance that it will be roughly neutral...)* If that expected value calculation comes out **very** negative \-- if the person can reasonably expect that their future will contain far more bad things than good things \-- then it's not obvious that the person is making a prudential mistake in taking their own life. (It might be a *moral* mistake, depending on what one thinks about the ethics of suicide. *Can a person morally mistreat themselves? Do other people have a moral claim against me that I continue living, for their sake?* etc.) **But most people who contemplate suicide are very unlike the ideal rational agent.** Almost by definition, a person with temporary but severe depression is not going to do a great job assessing the relative probabilities of different possible futures or in evaluating how good or bad those different possible outcomes will be. (e.g. maybe the person's mother just died. That person may, in his grief, overestimate how painful his mother's death will feel in 20 years.)

What that means, i think, is that suicidal people will very often *think* they have correctly calculated the expected value of their future but will, in fact, have botched the job. Killing themselves will be a prudential mistake, given what they should reasonably expect about their future. 

The case I gave in lecture was carefully designed to avoid this problem. First, I stipulated that the man's future contains mostly torture. (thus, the expected value calculate comes out with a big negative number.) **And** I had another person dispassionately making the expected value calculation for the prisoner. (Thus, avoiding the worries about depression)

(ii)

Maybe we think, *Suicide is a mistake b/c even if you firmly expect that your life is going to go badly, you could be wrong\! We can’t be 100% sure about such things. And, if you are wrong, you will have deprived yourself of mostly good things.*

It's true that one cannot ever be 100% confident about the future. Even if I have really good evidence that the rest of my life will suck hard, I can't be **100% sure** that it will. Things could turn out great\! And, if so, it will turn out to have been bad for me to kill myself.

But, notice, the exact same argument works in the other direction. Even if I have really good evidence that the rest of my life will be super dope, I can't be **100% sure** that it will. Things could turn out terribly\! And, if so, it will turn out to have been a bad for me not to have killed myself.

This is, simply, always true when making decisions under uncertainty. We mostly don't have guarantees about how the future will go, so any decisions we make are *risky.* Sometimes things look grim but turn out ok; other times things look good but turn out terribly. 

If the possibility of being wrong about what will probably happen in the future makes suicide always irrational, then, seemingly, *all* decisions under future-uncertainty will turn out to be irrational. It's always true (by def) in those cases, that the future could surprise us.

Thoughts about any that? (you or anyone else\!)

## Doesn’t the Deprivation Account say that death is good sometimes?
**Lecture:** The Badness of Death

I think this is exactly right\! Which is to say, I think you've accurately described the implications of the Deprivation Account.

Since we were trying to make sense of the intuitive *badness* of death, I focused on the part of the view where we talk about all the good things we miss out on by dying. But the view, strictly speaking, doesn't say that death is good or bad for people *tout court.* Rather it says, as you point out, that death causes people to miss out on the things in their future. If those were mostly good things, then their death is bad for them; if those were mostly bad things, then their death is good for them. (And if the bad and the good roughly cancel out, their death is neither bad nor good.)

I tend to think that these implications are a feature of the Deprivation Account, rather than a bug. These implications help me make sense of my intuitions that it's sometimes *a good thing* for a person to die \- or even a good thing, in a sense, for some people never to be born. In particular, I tend to think that people whose futures contain mostly pain and suffering may rationally want their lives to end early; those people aren't confused or making a mistake about whether an early death would be good for them. The D.A. seems to explain that judgment of mine and in a satisfying way.

That's somewhat controversial, of course. Indeed, the sorts of arguments you sometimes hear against euthanasia are going to be relevant here, too. (e.g. "Our lives are a sacred gift from God, and it is sinful and ungrateful to try to end them early / want them to end early.") But, then again, the sorts of people who give arguments like that tend not to believe in death, in the permanent cessation of persons. And so, for them, the Deprivation Account is a bit like an account of why unicorns have curved horns \-- i.e. an attempt to explain something that doesn't exist and so doesn't need explaining.
