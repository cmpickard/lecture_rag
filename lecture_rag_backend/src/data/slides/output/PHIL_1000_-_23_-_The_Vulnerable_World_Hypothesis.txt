“ One way of looking at human creativity is as a process of pulling balls out of a giant urn. The balls represent possible ideas, discoveries, technological inventions. Over the course of history, we have extracted a great many balls—mostly white (beneficial) but also various shades of grey (moderately harmful ones and mixed blessings). The cumulative effect on the human condition has so far been overwhelmingly positive, and may be much better still in the future… What we haven’t extracted, so far, is a black ball: a technology that invariably or by default destroys the civilization that invents it. The reason is not that we have been particularly careful or wise in our technology policy.  We have just been lucky. ” – Nick Bostrom

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Bostrum offers us a helpful metaphor for thinking about this process – i.e. the process of discovering and developing and employing new technologies.

White Balls Net good techs Grey Balls Net neutral to moderately bad techs Black Balls Catastrophically bad techs Bostrom – We’ve been lucky not to have pulled out a black ball.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
(i) the easy nukes case is an example of a Type 1 black ball. (ii) if we discovered or invented some super-virus – a disease that spreads quickly and can kill hundreds of millions – that would be a Type 1 black ball. The concern would be that just one instance in which that virus fell into the wrong hands would prob be enough for catastrophe (iii) another kind of Type 1 black ball could involve some system that is critical to the global economy and also really vulnerable to sabotage. If, say, there were some easy way to bring down the global banking industry. Or some easy way to destroy all the world’s wheat and rice crops. Or some easy way to bring down the internet. Any of those cases would be Type 1 vulnerabilities.


SLIDE 41

[SLIDE BODY]
Type 2a | The Threatened State Powerful actors have access to a devastating tech and have strong incentives to use it EX| A safe, first strike between super-powers A defensive, desperate strike by a small state A protectionist strike by an economic coalition ???

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Type 1 black balls are technologies that extremists might use to destroy the world. Type 2a black balls don’t require extremists. These are devastative technologies that could be used by basically rational agents – i.e. people who aren’t crazy and don’t have bizarre world views. In particular, Type 2a black balls involve superpowers, threatened states, or threatened super-groups. Basically, there could be cases where one of these groups has strong incentives to employ a technology defensively, but where doing so would lead to global devastation. At the beg. of the Cold War, the U.S and the Soviets had little ability to counter-strike if hit by a nuclear missile. Basically, whoever had nuked the other first would have won the Cold War AND would have emerged relatively unscathed. That gave both countries an incentive to strike first. (since that was the best way to guarantee safety) Suppose the U.S. is militarily dominating Ecuador. If E. has access to a deadly pathogen – and if they can innoculate their own population – releasing that might be their best bet to survive the war Suppose, like, OPEC is worried about Nuclear Fusion coming online.They might have good reason to cause nuclear meltdowns in all the fusion reactors to protect their economic dominance of the energy market.

Type 2b | Supersize Tragedy of the Commons A technology that lots of individuals have incentives to adopt but that, in the aggregate, causes devastation.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Type 2b black balls are also about incentives. Type 2a black balls involve technologies that non-crazy big states might have incentives to use. Type 2b techs are ones that lots of normal, non-extremist individuals might have incentives to use. There could be some technologies that are only devastating when millions or billions of individuals use them. Tragedy of the Commons Members of a pop. face some choice ( to X or not to X ) such that, (a) a very bad collective outcome (O) results from lots of people X-ing , (b) each individual does better for herself by X-ing , (c) no individual thinks her decision determines whether O occurs. Less formally, a tragedy of the commons is a situation in which each individual has self-interested incentives to X , but bad things happen when everyone X’s.

X = Precautionary Antibiotic Use O = Antibiotic-resistant bacteria

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Members of a pop. face some choice (to use Antibiotics every time they feel sick) but a very bad collective outcome will result if lots of people do that. (namely, antibiotic resistant bacteria emerge and lots of people die from bacterial infections that would previously have been preventable). What makes this a tragedy of the commons is that: (a) each individual does better for herself by using antibiotics frivolously (they may occasionally prevent an illness and they get peace of mind.) (b) no individual thinks that her decision determines whether antibiotic resistant bacteria emerge and kill lots of people. (antibiotic resistance only emerges as the macro-level when millions of people are doing this.)

Maybe there’s a small chance that they turn on us in a massive global uprising. Each individual may reason, I do best when I have a robot servant, even though my servant will add to the horde of evil robots in the case of an uprising. But what’s one more evil robot among millions? My choice doesn’t affect the outcome much.
