[Slide 19] The ETU

[Slide 20] Imagine a plausible future where human workers have mostly been replaced by non-humans – such that most of us are unable to find alternative work . work =df the performance of some act/skill in return for economic reward (or hope of receiving same) Call this the, Era of Technological Unemployment or ( ETU ) Let’s suppose that’s how things actually work out.

[Slide 21] Question Would it be bad for us to live in an ETU ?

[Slide 22] An Obvious Concern That would be terrible. Everyone would be desperately poor. As things now stand, it looks pretty likely that the future will be one with lots of income inequality. All those unemployed people will wind up desperately poor – because they don’t have any income! – while the Jeff Bezos’ who displaced them wind up unimaginably rich. That would be a terrible outcome.

[Slide 23] “ I simply assume that the distributional problem can be solved: the technologically unemployed future will be one of abundance, not deprivation. This is a significant assumption. It means that everything I say below is conditional on the assumption that people won’t be suffering from great hardship due to the lack of an income. ” – John Dahaner But that’s not the concern that Dahaner wants to talk about. He wants to know whether it would be good for us to live in an an ETU if we all had adequate access to resources

[Slide 24] Question Would it be bad for us to live in an ETU ( assuming we had adequate access to resources?) OR Is it intrinsically bad to be unemployed? Quiz Temperature check – how are you leaning right now?

[Slide 25] On the value of work

[Slide 26] “ In [our] contemporary economic-political [situation:] work is something that one must do in order to access the basic necessities and luxuries that make life possible and worth living. In other words, in the contemporary economy, work is intrinsically compulsory in nature. [The claims] is not that we are wage-slaves. We are not physically or legally obliged to work. Rather, work (and the pursuit of work) is a practical and economic necessity , even in the absence of physical or legal obligation. ” – John Dahaner Why?

Since work is the place that most adults spend the preponderance of their time, most adults are primarily only excellent at things that they practice a lot in their work.

[Slide 36] Danaher’s Argument

[Slide 37] Dahaner’s Argument (pt. 1) P1) In our era, the primary downside for people who’d prefer to not-work is that one needs to work to obtaining a number of critical human goods. P2) In an ETU , one won’t need to work to obtain those critical human goods [ P3) If the ETU eliminates the primary downside of not working, the ETU will be better for people who’d prefer to not-work] C) So in an ETU , people who’d prefer to not-work will be better off So those observations give rise to the first part of Dahaner’s argument: Think about those people who would, at the end of the day, really prefer not to work. For them, he argues, the ETU is probably better and at least no worse

[Slide 38] Dahaner’s Argument (pt. 2) P1) In an ETU , people will be able to choose to do work-like activities but won’t be forced to; in our era, people are effectively forced to. P2) All else equal, it’s better to choose to be in a circumstance, C, than to be forced into C. (even for those who’d ultimately prefer C anyway) But what about the people who DO want to work? Is the ETU bad for them? Again, Dahaner argues: No.

[Slide 42] Dahaner’s Argument P1) If the ETU would be bad for people, it must be bad for either the people who’d prefer to work or for the people who’d prefer not to work . (that’s all the people there are!) P2) The ETU won’t be bad for the people who’d prefer to work . (it’s better to choose to do work-like activities than to be forced to!) P3) The ETU won’t be bad for the people who’d prefer not to work . (they’ll be able to secure the same critical human goods, w/o needing jobs!) C) So the ETU won’t be bad for people.
