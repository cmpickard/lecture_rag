You go for a walk out in your neighborhood. Every few minutes, you turn around and see the same damn mailman walking slowly along behind you, apparently stopping at each house. What should you conclude abotu what’s going on? It’s irrational to conclude that the mailman is stalking you and that’s because we have a lot of background information about how mailmen work: it’s their job to walk slowly around the neighborhood. Thus, when you see a mailman doing just that, the reasonable thing to conclude is that she’s doing her job, and not that she’s stalking you. In this casem, it seems like you made a serious error in judgment .

Sometimes our disagreement is with an epistemic peer (i.e. someone about as competent / likely as you to get the right answer in this case.)

[PRESENTER NOTES]
You and your friend discover that you disagree about what it means to say that there’s a “north” or “northerly” wind blowing. You think that a north wind blows to the north. You are roughly the same age and of roughly equal intelligence. (YEP! You’re camping.) In this case, I think it’s clear that neither of you is the inferior or superior of the other. You seem to be roughly equally likely, equally well-equipped to have the right answer about this. Thus, we say that you two are epistemic peers.

[SLIDE BODY]
The Argument from Self-trust (for the Steadfast View) P1) If Conciliationism is true, it’s irrational to trust in myself over others. P2) Surely it’s not irrational to trust myself over others. C) So, surely Conciliationism is false.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
(P1) According to Conciliationism, the only rational thing to do in a case of peer disagreement is to place roughly as much trust in our own faculties as we do in the faculties of others – it tells us to conciliate between the equally-trustworthy positions. That is to say, Conciliationism tells us that our own views carry no extra weight. (P2) But you might think that’s wrong. You might think Conciliationism demands an objectionable kind of self-denial or self-abnegation. It treats my own views, my own beliefs as fragile things that must be tossed away at the first sign of contention. It asks that we be servile to the views of others, to be spineless in the face of disagreement. On the contrary, we might thin, when it comes to disagreement, we should stick to our guns because they’re our guns . The fact that it was me who reasoned to this conclusion – that fact that it’s my belief in question – surely does give me a reason to hold on to it, to trust myself to get it right.)


SLIDE 34
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Objection #1 There must be limits to self-trust! A person who self-trusts all the time is basically an epistemic monster. So the Steadfaster owes us a story about how much self-trust is rational .

[PRESENTER NOTES]
It is clearly not rational for me to refuse to accept my neighbor’s testimony in the vomitorium case merely out of self-trust. Nor, in general, is it rational to self-trust when disagreeing with an epistemic superior. Picture someone who you think is wrong about everything. Now imagine that person trumpeting the virtue of self-trust the next time you get into an argument with them – as though that were a reason to dismiss you. Imagine that person being wholly self-confident and self-righteous, unmoved by argument or reasons, completely dismissive of alternative viewpoints. Now imagine a world full of such people. Clearly that world would be a dogmatic, partisan nightmare. Most people are wrong about lots of stuff – there are, after all, more ways to be wrong than to be right. So it can’t be right that self-trust is the final word about disagreement. At the very least, the self-trust argument proponent owes us a story about how much extra weight we should be giving our own conclusions – not too much or we become monsters, not too little or this is just the Conciliationist position.


SLIDE 35
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Objection #2 It’s confused to call Conciliationism an enemy of self-trust. The question isn’t whether to trust my own judgments but which to trust : Judgment #1: P is true. Judgment #2: You are my epistemic peer.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Judgment #1 leads me to believe that P. Judgment #2 - when coupled to the fact that you think P is false - leads me not to believe that P. So… which judgment is more important? Should I trust myself and go with judgment #1 or trust myself and go with judgment #2? Of course, if I trust Judgment #1, I am thereby mistrusting my second judgment that you are an epistemic peer. In keeping my original degree of confidence, I am treating you like an epistemic inferior! On the other hand, if i trust Judgment #2 and I change my credence in light of our disagreement, then clearly I am mis- trusting my first judgment about the truth of P. Either way, I am both trusting myself and mis-trusting myself. THus, it is simply false to say the Conciliationism means a rejection of self-trust.

[SLIDE BODY]
We have 2 equally reliable thermometers. You check one; I check one. Mine reads 71.1 degrees Yours reads 74.1 degrees What should I believe about the temp? Surely, I should conciliate . I should think the two temps equally likely.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
It would be irrational for me to believe the temperature is 70. We agree that the thermometers are equally reliable! So why would I trust one more than the other? (Think about how weird it would be for me to insist that the temperature is 70 – as though it mattered that that’s the one I checked. I’m not better at reading digital thermometers than you. And it’s not as though I made a special choice when deciding which thermometer to go check. I could equally well have decided to check the other one.)

SLIDE 39
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
You and I are equally good at converting from C to F ° in their heads. Starting with the same temp in Celsius, we both do the maths. I calculate 71.1° F You calculate 74.1° F What should I believe about the temp? Again, surely I should conciliate . I should think the two temps equally likely.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Again, it seems like it would be irrational for me to believe the temperature is 71.1. We agree that we two are equally good at math! So why would I trust one of us more than the other? It’s like the thermometer case again, but the instrumental is in my head rather than outside it. But why should that matter? And it’s not obvious why I should privilege the instrument in my head merely because it’s mine. That seems weird! That seems as weird as trusting the thermometer that I checked merely because I’m the one who checked (and not because I have any special reason to think that it’s more reliable.) Or as weird as trusting the friend I asked merely because I asked her. At the point where you and I agree that we are both equally reliable at mental math, it’s really bizarre - seemingly irrational! – to then treat the other person’s calculator as worse than our own.

The Thermometer Analogy (an argument for Conciliationism) P1) If the Steadfast View is true, it’s rational in the mental math case to trust myself more than you. P2) It’s not rational in the mental math case to trust myself more than you. (that’s like trusting my thermometer more than yours.) C) So, the Steadfast View is false.
