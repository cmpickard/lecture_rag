The Greatest Being God God is the greatest possible being – the being than which none greater can be conceived .

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Here’s the definition that we’ll be working with in the Ontological Argument. 2 points need to made here. By “great”, we mean “better” or “more admirable” or “more perfect” – God is the perfectest being. Also bear in mind that what’s relevant here is possibility, rather than actuality. So the class of things than which God is greater is all the possible things; all the things not in category 3, in other words.

Of those 6 options, only 3 of them seem actively viable. P1 seems like a logical truth. (Assuming we’ve done our possible being metaphysics correctly) P3 & P5 are both definitions. It typically doesn’t make sense to say that a definition is false, unless the definition is reporting on the world – unless the definition has an empirical truth value. The def in P3 is a logical entailment. An analytic truth. Nothing to argue with there. What about P5? In general I think, here and in other cases, it just doesn’t make a ton of sense to argue with a definition.

C1) An R.L. is not a Category 3 entity P3) By definition, Category 2 beings don’t have the property of existence P4) By definition, an R.L. is a leprechaun that actually exists. So…. obviously that’s bullshit. Something’s gone wrong. This argument is defective. But WHY? What’s the defect? Where did we screw up?

The mistake seems to be this. We also know they aren’t in Category 1 . (SPOILER: Leprechauns don’t exist) Thus, they’re impossible. R.L.s are in Category 3 . By definition, real-leprechauns don’t belong in Category 2 .

[PRESENTER NOTES]
It helps to try to assemble our facts about Real Leprechauns in a different order. Fact #1: Real Leprechauns are by definition not in Category 2 Fact #2: There are no leprechauns in reality. So they aren’t in Category 1 Conclusion: Real Leprechauns are in Category 3: the are impossible beings.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Look at premise 2. It looks innocent, but it’s not. Since R.L. are either real or impossible, by definition, when we accept P2 be automatically grant that Real-leprechauns exist. We shouldn’t do that –  That’s a mistake! Real-leprechauns are, apparently, impossible. (That’s weird and surprising. They don’t seem impossible! But they are. Because they aren’t real. And they aren’t merely-possible by definition. Thus, the way that we’ve defined real leprechaun forces them into Category 3. It’s not obvous that we’ve done that, just by glancing at the definition, but it’s true. Weird!


SLIDE 43
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
So too with the Ontological Arg. By Anselm’s def, God isn’t merely possible . (at least, if we accept that existence is great-making .) That is, by definition God is either Category 1 or Category 3 . (either real or impossible) But then, we should not accept P2 . It looks innocent, but it’s not. (b/c Anselm’s definition is sneaky .)

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Anselm is saying, “Let’s define God as real or impossible. Since we can imagine God, God is not impossible. So, She’s real.”


SLIDE 44
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
P1) God is either a Category 1 , a Category 2 , or a Category 3 entity. P2) God is not logically impossible (is not Category 3 ) P3) By definition, God is not Category 2 C) So, God is in Category 1 , is real The Ontological Argument (un-sneaky’d)

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Not a good argument! No one should accept P2, given the definition in P3. What the R.L. case teaches us is that just because we can imagine something doesn’t mean that thing is possible. We seem to be able to imagine Real Leprechauns, even though they are impossible. (b/c they don’t actually exist and by definition aren’t merely possible.) Likewise, just because we seem to be able to imagine God as the greatest possible being, that doesn’t mean God – as Anselm defines Her – isn’t impossible.
