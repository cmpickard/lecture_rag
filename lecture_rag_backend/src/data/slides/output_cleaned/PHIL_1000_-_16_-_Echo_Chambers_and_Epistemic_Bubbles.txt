[Slide 32] Echo chamber =df a social structure in which relevant, outside voices are actively discredited.

[Slide 33] Your mother has pushed you into an echo chamber . As long as you can remember, your mom’s been telling you that everyone in the world – everyone but you and her – is a robot. You two are the only real people in a sea of malicious automatons. She’s warned you, “Don’t let your guard down and don’t let yourself get fooled. They’re gonna act like people. They might tell you that they are people. They might get mad if you let on that you know. (“Hey! Fuck you! I’m not robot!”) But that’s how they get you. It’s just you and me, kiddo.”

[Slide 34] Your mother has pushed you into an echo chamber . How will you react when other people try to tell you you’re wrong? Presumably, you’ll entirely dismiss them. You’ll think ‘Nice try, you robot bastard’ You might even increase your level of confidence that everyone else is a robot. (Which is pretty messed up, when you think about it. It’s weird to respond to someone telling you “Hey I’m not a robot!” by becoming more confident that they are, in fact, a robot.)

[Slide 39] Echo Chambers form mostly by design. Someone has to argue you into the chamber : “Our Team is good and right and trustworthy. Their Team is bad and wrong and lying.”

[Slide 40] All else equal, the order in which you receive evidence should be rationally irrelevant – shouldn’t affect your final credences. Echo chambers arise when people mistake earlier evidence for better evidence. C. Thi Nguyen More than that, he says, the mistake is this.

[Slide 41] Case #1 : Your mom spends 18 years telling you that everyone is a robot, starting from when you’re 2 . Other people all insist that they’re not robots. Case #2 : Your mom spends 18 years telling you that everyone is a robot, starting from when you’re 20 . Other people all insist that they’re not robots. Recall the robot case from earlier. You have the same evidence in each case. But only in one of them do you get stuck in an echo chamber. (This is largely because children are very susceptible to this mistake: treating earlier evidence as better evidence.)

[Slide 48] So… why should we reject the Post-truth Thesis?

[Slide 49] “ Listen to what it actually sounds like when people reject the plain facts – it doesn’t sound like brute irrationality. One side points out a piece of economic data; the other side rejects that data by rejecting its source. They think that newspaper is biased, or the academic elites generating the data are corrupt… In many ways, echo-chamber members are following reasonable and rational procedures of enquiry. They are engaging in critical reasoning... It’s simply that their basis for evaluation – their background beliefs about whom to trust – are radically different. They are not irrational, but systematically misinformed about where to place their trust.” – C. Thi Nguyen

[Slide 50] P1) If the Post-Truth Thesis is true, we should observe brute irrationality – i.e. a complete disregard for evidence, reasoning, and critical thinking. P2) We do not observe brute irrationality. C) So the Post-Truth Thesis is false. Nguyen’s argument against the PTT

[Slide 51] “ Echo chambers don’t trade in vague, ambiguous pseudo-speech. We should expect [rather, that those in] echo chambers would deliver crisp, clear, unambiguous claims about who is trustworthy and who is not. And this… is exactly what we find: clearly articulated conspiracy theories, and crisply worded accusations of an outside world rife with untrustworthiness and corruption.” – C. Thi Nguyen

[Slide 52] P1) If the Echo Chamber explanation is correct, we will observe partisans rejecting outsiders by appealing to conspiracies and corruption. P2) We do in fact observe this. C) So the Echo Chamber explanation is correct Nguyen’s argument for the Echo Chamber explanation
