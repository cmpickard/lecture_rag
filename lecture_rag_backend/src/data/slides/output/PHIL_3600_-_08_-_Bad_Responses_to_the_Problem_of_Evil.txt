Some kinds of suffering are necessary . The problem of evil is about unnecessary suffering . Suffering is unnecessary when it doesn’t, (a) prevent some greater bad NOR (b) secure some greater good.

Recall this VERY IMPORTANT distinction between necessary suffering and unnecessary suffering. The Problem of Evil is only concerned with Unnecessary Suffering. (Thus, the existence of vaccines or dentists or exercise is not being used as evidence against the existence of god.)

We must argue, There has never been – not even once – an instance of unnecessary suffering.

SLIDE 10
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
The conversation goes like this… Atheist: “What about X? Doesn’t X count as unnecessary suffering?” Theist: “No. Plausibly, we need X in order to prevent <some greater evil> or to secure <some greater good.>” Atheist: “But what about Y? Doesn’t Y count as unnecessary suffering?” Theist: “No. Plausibly, we need Y…” This is a theodicy.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
In English, we have a special word for this kind of story. It’s called a “theodicy”. A theodicy is an explanation that a certain variety of suffering is actually necessary (and, thus, isn’t evidence against the existence of God.)

What about ass hair ? Is ass hair an example of unnecessary suffering? Theodicy: No. Body hair is useful in areas that are hard to keep dry. We need ass hair (as we need armpit hair) to wick sweat away from spots that doesn’t get much air exposure.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
We did a little practice with this last lecture, but it’s worth trying our hand at it again. So, here’s an example. Maybe the atheist says, “Look at ass hair! It’s kinda gross. It’s kinda messy. It’s an extra place to shed from. No one really likes it. Surely ass hair is an instance of unnecessary suffering.” The theist might then give a theodicy , a plausible story the can explain why ass hair might be a necessary evil ... I don’t know if that’s true of course, but the point is that it’s plausible. This is a reasonably satisfying story about the necessity of ass hair. I’m content to let this one go – to not insist that ass hair is evidence that God doesn’t exist.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
I should make one important note before we look at some theodicies: The challenge for the theist isn’t merely to show that SOME war or SOME disease is necessary. That’s not enough. The Problem of Evil says that “there has been at least one instance of necessary suffering.” That’s it. At least one. So consider torture. Sometimes people will respond to this case by arguing that sometimes torture is the right thing to do to prevent a greater evil – like in a ticking bomb scenario or whatever. But… so what? Even if that’s true, that’s not nearly enough. To remove torture from this list, the theist has to show that every act of torture that’s ever been committed by anyone anywhere was a necessary evil. Which… yeah. That’s gonna be hard. There’s been a lot of torture-for-fun in the history of mankind. Gonna be hard to explain those cases away as necessary evil. And of course, similar remarks apply to our other examples. The theists needs to explain ALL wars, ALL genocides, ALL natural disasters – not just some wars, genocides, or natural disasters.

SLIDE 17
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
The God’s Plan Theodicy Objection #1 This isn’t a solution. It’s a restatement of the problem. Objection #2 This theodicy commits some circular reasoning .

[PRESENTER NOTES]
The problem is 2-fold This theodicy isn’t an explanation; it’s just a restatement of the question but posed as an answer. It’s true that if the 3-O God exists, then her plan is perfect and all suffering is necessary. But that’s not a reason to think that the 3-O God does exist nor a reason to believe that there exists a perfect plan. The question is Can we make sense of all the suffering in the world as necessary? And this theodicy amounts to the answer, No. We cannot make sense of suffering. We are too stupid to understand his plan, which is why the world clearly appears to include lots of unnecessary suffering. But, hopefully you can see, that’s not a solution! That’s quitting! (also, the atheist at this point might simply reply, “Isn’t our stupidity ANOTHER example of unnecessary evil? Surely a 3-O God could have made us smart enough to understand her morally perfect plan? The fact that we’re too stupid to understand Her perfect plan is another instance of unnecessary evil.”) The second problem with this theodicy is that it’s flagrantly circular. We ask, “Is God’s plan a good plan?” The theist answers, Yes it must be perfect because God is morally perfect. We ask, “Well, why should we think God is morally perfect?” The theist replies, Just look at the evidence! Everything around us is perfect! There is nothing out of place; everything in accordance with God’s plan. We reply, “Uhhh… mostly what I see is suffering. So... our evidence actually suggests it’s a bad plan.” The theist replies, No it’s a good plan! It’s God’s plan and She’s morally perfect! And so on. The theist never actually manages to explain anything at all.

[SLIDE BODY]
Objection #1 How does this even help us understand the moral perfection of God’s plan? Objection #2 If anything, this response makes human suffering sound positively unjust .

[PRESENTER NOTES]
The doctrine of Original Sin isn’t the right sort of story. It tells us what caused there to be suffering. It doesn’t tell us why all the suffering is justified. It doesn’t make sense to say “All suffering is necessary because of Adam and Eve and the apple.” That’s like saying “Yes, I burned down your house, but it’s not my fault b/c fire was invented thousands of years ago.” If we try to force the doctrine of Original Sin into the right sort of story (i.e. into a justification of suffering) the result is genuinely repulsive. That theodicy says, it is morally perfect for all humanity to suffer and suffer and suffer because one time, thousands of years ago, a couple people did a bad thing with some fruit. Since that bad thing, is has been morally perfect for God to stand back and watch the genocides.”
