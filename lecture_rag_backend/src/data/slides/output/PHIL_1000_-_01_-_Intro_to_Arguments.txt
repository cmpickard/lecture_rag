[SLIDE BODY]
Proposition =df a statement that bears a truth-value – either true or false You can think of propositions as sentences that claim something about the world – about the way it is, was, will be, should be, or might be. A proposition is TRUE just in case the world actually is (was/will be) that way. A proposition is FALSE just in case the world is NOT that way.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
By “the world” I just mean, like, REALITY. The universe and everything that actually exists in it. You’ll notice, there’s nothing particular spooky or mystical or special about Truth on this definition. That’s a good thing! To say that something is “true” is just to say that there is a correspondence between what we say about the world and the way the world actually is.

[SLIDE BODY]
Argument =df a collection of propositions, some of them (the premises ) being offered as evidence for the truth of another one of them (the conclusion ) In essence, a good argument is one that does a good job making its conclusion seem true.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
That’s what an argument is. But in this course, one of our primary tasks will be assessing arguments. So we should ask: What makes an argument a good one?


SLIDE 22
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
The two most common metrics of goodness are validity and soundness An argument is valid iff the truth of the premises is sufficient for (or guarantees ) the truth of the conclusion. An argument is sound iff it is valid and has true premises.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
These are genuinely tough concepts. But they’re also pretty important. Put differently, a valid argument is one’s whose premises were they true, would guarantee the truth of the conclusion. N.B.: That means, a valid argument can have false premises! Validity isn’t about truth or falsity per se. It’s about the relationship between the truth of the premises and the truth of the conconclusion. This can be a bit challenging to wrap one’s head around, so we’ll look at a bunch of examples. Soundness is easier than validity. Soundness is just validity + true premises. (thus, all invalid argument are unsound) Soundness is, hopefully you can see, basically the metric of success. If an argument is sound, then you should accept its conclusion. (If you agree that an argument is sound, you agree that all its premises are true and that the premises guarantee the truth of the conclusion. Thus, you must accept the conclusion.)

[SLIDE BODY]
An Elaborate Analogy Imagine you live in a town surrounded on all sides by manure lagoons (i.e. big pits where livestock feces is stored.) Your town needs fresh, clean water, but all the available water sources are on the far side of the manure lagoons. So pipes must be constructed to carry water from a distant spring, through the pits, to the town. Obviously, you won’t be willing to drink the water unless you’re sure that the pipes are perfectly tight, perfectly shit-excluding, and perfectly good at preserving the cleanliness of the water. That’s not enough, though! For the town’s water to be drinkable, you also need assurances that the original spring water actually is clean. (After all, if you put nasty water in the World’s Best Pipes, you’ll get nasty water out the other end.) In short, good, drinkable water requires both good pipes and good source water.
