[PRESENTER NOTES]
The history of philosophy also supports this way of thinking about the discipline – especially the contrast with science. If you go back and look at ancient Greek philosophy, you’ll quickly notice that a lot of it is actually proto-physics. People like aristotle and Epicurus (who was one of the first to believe in atoms) were all trying to do physics by just, like, thinking really hard. Then, eventually, we realized that you can learn about physics better by doing science about it, by conducting experiments. Thus, physics splintered off from philosophy and became it’s own scientific discipline. (For the most part…) Philosophers no longer do physics from the armchair, and physicists are no longer doing philosophy.


SLIDE 18
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
(no text on this slide)

[PRESENTER NOTES]
We see this same pattern in the history of Psychology. Until around the turn of the 20th century, most of the best psychologists were just philosophers: they were trying to figure out how the human mind works by just thinking about it really hard. But, again, we eventually realized that psychology can be studied scientifically – with experimentation and carefully manipulated real-world observation. Thus, psychology splintered off from philosophy. It became it’s own discipline. (For the most part…) philosophers are no longer doing psychology, and psychologists are no longer considered philosophers.
