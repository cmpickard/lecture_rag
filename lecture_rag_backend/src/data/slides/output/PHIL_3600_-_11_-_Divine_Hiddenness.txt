When we say, “God loves us” We mean , God feels about us the same way we feel about our most cherished companions . (though, God’s love is maybe stronger than ours.)

[PRESENTER NOTES]
... That’s important. God’s love is supposed to be, essentially, the same feeling as OUR love. We don’t use scare-quotes when we say that God “loves” us. It’s not supposed to be a metaphor or a gross oversimplification. It’s not as though God is feeling something completely alien and un-human towards us, a feeling we can only crudely approximate with the word ‘love’. No. God loves like we love – though, probably even better.Indeed, the story we get (at least from Abrahamic religions) is that god made us in Her image. Love was her feeling first, and she made us in such a way that we’re capable of that feeling too.

Imagine that you come across a child in a dark forest. He’s alone and clearly terrified, calling out repeatedly for his mother. You ask him what the matter is and if you can help. “I need to find my mother, so we can leave the forest before dark,” he says. “She’s here in the forest with you?” you ask. “Yeah. "If all that’s true, then why hasn’t she come for you?” The boy just shrugs. “Dunno.” He turns away and begins calling out for his mother again.

SLIDE 22
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
The faithful often seek out God for comfort, companionship, or understanding – but come up empty.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
As I pointed out a few minutes ago, this is a very well-documented phenomenon. Lots of theists report experiencing this at some point in their religious lives. They earnestly reach for some connection to God, some shred of her presence and love, and they find nothing. If God is perfectly loving, that is very puzzling. That absence cries out for explanation. Why is God not more present?

[SLIDE BODY]
P1) If the 3-O God exists, there could never be even one instance of unnecessary absence . (b/c God loves us, knows about our needs, wants what’s best for us, and has the power to be with us.) P2) There has been at least one instance of unnecessary divine absence . C) The 3-O God does not exist The Problem of Divine Hiddenness

[PRESENTER NOTES]
As it happens, the easiest way to formalize the argument from divine hiddenness is to mirror our original reasoning from the problem of evil. We point out in P1) that we expect certain things to be true of our world, if there is a loving 3-O God: Given God’s nature, it seems impossible for the 3-O God to exist and for the world to contain unnecessary absences . We then point out in P2) that it sure does look like God has been unnecessarily absent at least once.

[SLIDE BODY]
An evil dictator is building a new extermination camp for his political enemies. He believes he’s doing God’s work, and he prays to God for guidance on where to locate the extermination camp. God gives no response.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
This is an instance of hiddenness. There is an earnest attempt by the dictator to find God, and God is nowhere to be found. This is a case of divine absence – b ut, hopefully you can see, this case is easy for the theist to deal with. This case is presumably not good evidence against the 3-O God b/c the theist can tell a plausible story according to which God’s absence is necessary, is needed to prevent some greater evil. Maybe, e.g., we can say: God doesn’t respond to the dictator b/c God doesn’t want to endorse the extermination camp. And, perhaps, when the dictator doesn’t hear anything from God, he will be able to infer that God doesn’t want him to build an extermination camp at all. Thus: God is not just hiding; She is withholding her approval. (Just as a loving mother might in the same situation!)

[SLIDE BODY]
A young woman has procrastinated writing a paper for months. It’s due tomorrow, and she hasn’t even read the book she’s supposed to write about. She prays to God for aid and comfort and finds none.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Here’s another case of divine hiddenness. There is an earnest attempt by the young woman to find God, and God is nowhere to be found. But this case too is pretty easy for the theist to deal with: it’s not hard to tell a plausible story according to which God’s absence is needed to prevent some greater evil. Maybe we say, God wants this young woman to learn from her mistake and to be more assiduous in future. But in order for her to learn from her mistake, the mistake needs to hurt at least a little bit. That’s why God is withholding her comfort – just as a loving mother might in the same situation.

[SLIDE BODY]

[PRESENTER NOTES]
… But this reply simply pushes the question back a step. Instead of ‘why so absent,’ now it’s ‘why so hard to find.’ It’s not obvious that question is any better. Put differently, this response doesn’t seem to actually help us at all. This merely asserts that God’s absences are necessary. But what’s needed is an explanation for why those absences are necessary.

----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Finally... Well, yes. Any solution to the problem will take this form. But notice the analogy with the problem of evil. The trick is to explain, in terms we can understand, why a good plan would involve hiddenness. Because from where we’re standing, it sure does look like a garbage plan – what with all the apparently unnecessary absences. Again, T=this merely asserts that God’s absences are necessary when what’s needed is an explanation for why those absences are necessary. The Atheist challenge is: Can you explain why God is so often absent? And this reply amounts to: No I cannot. But there must be an explanation. Which isn’t victory; it’s quitting.

There’s obviously a lot more that can be said here, but at this point, I think you can see that most of it will follow the same paths as the problem of evil. The theist needs some sort of theodicy sufficient for rejecting P2. Maybe they can point to Free Will. (“Surely we wouldn’t really be free if God were overbearingly present in our lives.”) Maybe they can point to soul-making. (“Surely we can only grow as people if we feel ourselves alone and required to be self-sufficient, without God’s comfort and aid.”)So that’s an open question that I’ll leave for this week’s discussions. What’s the best way for the theist to respond to the Problem of Divine Hiddenness? The other open question concerns the relationship between this Problem and the Problem of Evil. As we’ve seen, the two problems share a lot of the same essential features and are largely isomorphic. But that raises the question, What’s the relationship between the two? Is divine hiddenness just a specific sort of evil (meaning, therefore, that this problem is just a special case of the broader problem of evil)? Or maybe it’s better to reverse that: maybe all evil is best understood as a kind of divine absence? Or alternatively, maybe the two problems are more like brothers – related but not identical in whole or in part?
