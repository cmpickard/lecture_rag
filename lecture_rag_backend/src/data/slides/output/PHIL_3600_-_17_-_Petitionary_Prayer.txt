Most of these types of prayers are straightforwardly reasonable. In other words, they make sense for theists to perform , given their belief in a loving 3-O God. Praise is a little bit complicated since a loving 3-O God clearly doesn’t need our encouragement to keep going or our admiration to feel like She’s doing a good job (literally the best possible job, as we’ve already extensively discussed.). So, we might sensibly ask, what’s the point of our praising God? What role does praise serve in one’s spiritual life, given that God really, definitely doesn’t need our praise? SLIDE 6

[SLIDE BODY]
We are interested in these. People pray to, among other things, Affirm their devotion Voice their admiration Indicate their contrition Express their gratitude Request God’s intercession

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Our actual target is that last category of prayers, the prayers of intercession or, more simply put, petitionary prayers. Very often, people pray to God when and because they want something. They pray for aid for themselves, for their loved ones and acquaintances, or for the world more generally. A prayer for healing is a petitionary prayer. A prayer for one’s atheist neighbor to find god is a petitionary prayer. A prayer for the hurricane victims or for world peace is a petitionary prayer. In each case, the person praying is asking God for help, asking God to bring about some particular, desired state of affairs . As we’ll see, it’s actually rather difficult to make sense of these sorts of prayers when one believes in a loving, 3-O God.

[SLIDE BODY]
Do petitionary prayers make sense? (Are they effective or pointless and futile ?)

[PRESENTER NOTES]
This, then, is our question. For the most part, people make petitionary prayers to God because they genuinely want God’s aid AND, they seem to think, praying for it is an effective way to get help. So our question is:  Does that make sense? Is it plausibly true that making petitions to God, asking for GOd’s help, really works ?

For a petitionary prayer to make sense , the person praying must think that, God is able to help. God will want to help. God will not help unless a petition is made. Why would this ever be true?

[PRESENTER NOTES]
… When all of these things are true, then praying to God for help makes good sense. When any or all of these things are not true, praying to God for help won’t really make sense. Will be futile, pointless, silly, absurd. So imagine a theist who is praying to a loving 3-O God for aid. Surely Condition 1 is satisfied; surely God can help. God is all-powerful! And surely Condition 2 is satisfied; since God is loving and morally perfect, surely She wants to be of assistance for her chosen companions. (assuming the person isn’t asking for something evil.) But what about Condition 3?As we’ll see, Condition 3 is what gives rise to our problem. Simply put, it is hard to explain why Condition 3 would ever be true. Why would a loving 3-O God ever make her assistance conditional on a prayer?

Here’s a case of the opposite sort: Suppose you’re at the DMV to get your license renewed. You finally finish the process, and the employee working with says you owe $35.68. Here’s the worry: Petitioning a loving 3-O God for help is rather like petitioning a surgeon to wash their hands before surgery or petitioning a random DMV employee to re-name her dog after you. It’s hard to see how any of these requests could ever make a difference. Since God already knows everything and already has a perfect plan for the future, your petitions must surely all seem pointless and futile. There are 2 ways this might go: On the one hand, if you pray for something good and sensible, then God is obviously already aware of your need and obviously already planning to help. Your asking doesn’t make a difference. Obversely, if you’re asking God for something stupid or evil or impossible or just plain unwise or unworkable, then God has obviously already determined not to do that and your request is, again, completely pointless. Your asking cannot make a difference. Thus, either way – whether you ask for something sensible or wicked – surely your petition doesn’t make a difference. [next slide]

Responding to the Problem

[PRESENTER NOTES]
What options does the theist have for making sense of petitionary prayer, then?


SLIDE 27
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Possible Response God has already decided that we’ll pray AND that our prayers will be a meaningful part of the causal pattern of the world.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
... This is effectively Thomas Aquinas’ response to the problem. On this view, our petitionary prayers ARE NOT aimed at getting God to do something that She wouldn’t otherwise do. Rather the course of events is entirely predetermined and we have been assigned a role in those events: namely, our role is to offer prayers. God has woven our prayers into the causal fabric of the universe. Which means our question, “But do petitionary prayers actually DO anything?” doesn’t arise. God has already decided that there will be prayers . Praying is our assigned role. It’s true that our prayers for aid don’t persuade God . But they are still important.


SLIDE 28
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Replies (i) This doesn’t explain why our prayers are included in God’s plan as important causes. (ii) This is, essentially, just an assertion that there is no problem . (“ Pet. prayers make sense b/c God says they do!”) (iii) This seems to give up on free will .

[PRESENTER NOTES]
1) The original problem is that praying for assistance from God seems futile and pointless. It’s not obvious that this response really helps. Why should we bother praying? Aquinas tells us, because God has already planned for us to pray. But… why though? Why does God want that in her plans? Why doesn’t God tell us instead to stop wasting our damn time on prayers and go help the poor? In other words, this proposal doesn’t actually explain what we’re trying to explain. At best, we’ve merely pushed the question back a step. Our original question, “Why should we bother praying for help?”, has now been replaced with a new question, “Why does God want us to bother praying for help?” 2) We’ve seen this issue over and over again in this course. Hopefully, you’ve picked up on the pattern by now. First, we observe some apparent contradiction lurking among our religious beliefs. “What up with that?” we ask. Then one of the theist’s first responses is to say, “Ahh, but there’s actually no contradiction here b/c of God and the way that She is.” Which… isn’t really a response at all. Replies like that don’t give us any reasons for thinking that the theist is right. As before, as always, this response sucks. It’s unsatisfying. It’s quitting on the problem, not resolving it. 3) A final concern with this reply is that it seems to require that God preordain our petitionary prayers. Aquinas tells us that God has already decided that we will pray! We don’t really have a choice! In that sense, this “solution” at best only gets rid of our present problem to create another one. Now we can make sense of prayer, but we don’t have any free will.

----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Possible Response Maybe our petitionary prayers provide God with new information ? Reply This won’t work if God is truly Omniscient .

[PRESENTER NOTES]
What else might we say to defend the sensibleness of petitionary prayer? Well, recall, the problem before us is that petitionary prayers don’t seem to ever make a difference to what God decides to do. And, recall, there were two ways in which you might expect a petition to make a difference. First, your petition might give the person new information that they didn’t have before. Second, your petition might give the person new reasons to act that they didn’t have before. So, one option for making sense of petitionary prayer is to argue that our prayers provide God with new information. Our prayers matter and make sense b/c they apprize God of important new facts. But that won’t work. If God really does already and always know all of the facts, then we have nothing to teach Her.

[SLIDE BODY]
Possible Response Maybe our petitionary prayers provide God with new reasons to act?

[PRESENTER NOTES]
But what about the second way in which petitions make a difference? Maybe we don’t teach God anything, but, instead, we give Her new reasons. Maybe the mere fact of our asking gives God a new reason to act that She didn’t have before.


SLIDE 31

[SLIDE BODY]
Quiz

[PRESENTER NOTES]
The thought is something like this: Suppose you are a parent, and your child is struggling with their math homework. Do you immediately go to help them, as soon as you see them struggling? Probably not. You probably wait until they ask for your help. But why? Quiz question: Think about that for a second. Why might you hold off on helping your child with their math homework until they ask ? Lots of things we might say here: Maybe you hold off on helping b/c you’re trying to teach them independence. Or maybe they need to learn that sometimes we should ask others for the help we need. Or maybe your child simply prefers to work through hard problems on their own. And so when you offer to help w/o being asked, they resent it. (“I didn’t ask you yet!” your kid yells.) and thus, when your child does ask for help, you get a new reason to act that you didn’t have before: your child is ready for help. That wasn’t true until he asked!


SLIDE 32
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Can we tell the same story about God?

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Perhaps petitionary prayers are like that. Maybe God is trying to teach us independence, and so doesn’t rush to help us unasked. Or maybe God wants some of us to learn that we sometimes must ask for the help we need – and so doesn’t rush to help us unasked. Or maybe many of us simply prefer to work through hard problems on their own – and so God waits to be asked. On any of these stories, when a person does finally pray to God for help, God does thereby get a new reason to act that She didn’t have before: your child is ready for help. That wasn’t true until the prayer was made!

A couple possible worries about this response: (1) One concern: In the parent case, the child’s request is doing two things at once: it is both giving the parent new reason AND giving the parent new information. The child’s request informs the parent, Okay, I’m ready for help! But that’s not true in the God case. God will know when you’re ready for help as soon as you’re ready. You don’t need to tell God. She already knows. So… what good is your prayer doing? Why do we need to announce our readiness with petitionary prayers? God knows when we’re ready, and seemingly that’s when She gets new reasons to act. But then, again, what’s the point of prayer? (2)... Let me give you a case to illustrate the thought.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Imagine a small mountain town, nestled in a valley. Rains have been heavy this season and so has soil erosion. One day, two residents hear a loud rumbling. Running outside, they see a swarm of huge boulders, loosened by the rains, rolling down a ridge toward the town. One resident immediately throws up a prayer, “God, please spare my house and my family from the avalanche.” The other resident, not being a religious man, doesn’t pray. SLIDE 37
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Two ways this might have gone... Option 1 The prayer was irrelevant . God’s morally perfect plan was always to crush the one house and spare the other. Option 2 The prayer was relevant . God’s plan was, originally, to crush both houses, but the prayer prevented one of the crushings.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Now, obviously Option 1 does raise some serious concerns! Why would it ever be morally perfect for God to crush a house with a boulder? But, notice, that’s just the Problem of Evil – why would God let this bad thing happen? I don’t know, but we’ve already had that conversation. Nothing new here. Option 2, though, raises both Problem of Evil concerns and a genuinely new concern: If God answers prayers like this, if the theist’s prayers really do provide God with a new reason to act, then God now appears either unfair and vindictive. If prayers matter and make a difference, then people who don’t pray must thereby suffer more and suffer preventably and precisely because they don’t pray. On this view, it is part of God’s morally perfect plan that She will prevent bad things from happening to Her favorites at their request, but will let bad things befall everyone else. The non-believers are, effectively, punished for their failure to pray. (Obviously, God knows that the atheists need her help. But, on Option 2, She doesn’t actually help them unless they pray.) The point is: It’s very hard to imagine a morally perfect being adopting this strategy. God is supposed to love all us equally, to want what’s best for all of us all of the time. And it’s really hard to think that a being like that would punish people for not praying.

So that’s the second problem with this reply (i.e. the one that says “Petitionary Prayers give god new reasons that She didn’t have before.”) The problem is, briefly, that many of those who need help don’t ask God for it. So if God answers prayers, if prayers really do make a difference, there’s now a serious fairness problem. God apparently isn’t willing to count a person’s readiness for help as a good reason to act unless that person actually asks. And that seems pretty messed up.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Thus, our response… On this view, all petitionary prayers are just devotional prayers. The person who prays isn’t actually trying to persuade God to help; she’s just expressing her admiration for God and her confidence in God’s perfect plan. That’s helpful b/c it’s easy to make sense of devotional prayers. Those are clearly sensible and rational. It makes sense for theists to periodically renew their devotion to God and to remind themselves that everything that happens is in accordance with God’s plan. … What are we to make of this reply?I think this response is potentially successful , but comes at a moderate cost. The cost is that we must now admit that most theists are simply confused when they make petitionary prayers. Most theists actually do believe that their prayers for assistance are effective petitions. But if we adopt this response, that’s not correct. Our prayers for aid don’t make a difference; they simply affirm our devotion to God. So this response is maybe successful but highly revisionary – we have to revise our understand of WHY we pray for God’s assistance.

SLIDE 43
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Two other issues surrounding petitionary prayer (1) We can’t tell whether our prayers made any difference, when answered (2) We can’t tell why our prayers are unanswered, when they are

[PRESENTER NOTES]
We’ve talked about A problem with petitionary prayers. But there seem to be a couple other, philosophically interesting ones. Suppose we decide that petitionary prayers really are effective and really do sometimes make a difference. If so, then I think the theist is still left with two interesting epistemological problems. The first is that it’s never going to be perfectly clear whether our prayers made a difference. Suppose you get the thing you prayed for. Sometimes that may be because you prayed. Other times is may be because God was already planning to bring about whatever thing you prayed for. But how do we tell those cases apart? Second, there will be plenty of other times where the theist’s prayers clearly don’t get answered by God. This too raises an interesting problem. Namely, it’s rarely going to be obvious WHY God has chosen not to answer our prayers. Is it because the thing we’ve prayed for is wicked or selfish? Maybe it’s because it would lead to some greater evil that we ourselves don’t forsee? Maybe it’s b/c God is trying to teach us not to rely to heavily on Her? ANd how do we tell which cases are which? I don’t know. Hard questions.Ok. I’ll leave off here. Hopefully in the discussions this week, you all will be able to advance some of these issues a bit further.
