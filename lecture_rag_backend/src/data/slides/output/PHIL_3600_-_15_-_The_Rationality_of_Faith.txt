James suggests that mystical, religious experiences tend to have in common, Ineffability (i.e. seem indescribable in words) Noetic quality (i.e. seem like true insights into reality) Transiency (i.e. tend to pass in a few minutes or hours) Passivity (i.e. seem “forced upon” the experiencer)

SLIDE 27
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Mystical states , James observes, tend to be “absolutely authoritative over the individuals to whom they come.” That is, by their very nature, these experiences tend to strike people as obviously true and real .

[PRESENTER NOTES]
James makes a few final and important observations about religious experiences or mystial states… To be clear, this is merely descriptive. James is just telling how people actually seem to behave. He’s not saying that they’re being rational. (nor is he saying they’re being irrational. ) He’s merely pointing out that people who have very clear religious experiences tend to insist that those experiences are accurate. The experiences themselves often come pre-packaged with a forceful sense of veridicality. (the experience itself seems to arrive screaming, I am real; I am true; this is reality laid bare. Again, that’s just a description of how people actually talk about these experiences and act.

Let’s start with a non-religious case. Suppose you have a friend with a balloon fetish. One day, you’re both confronted with a balloon. She might say, “I’m having all the same perceptual experiences as you: I smell latex; I see a red, round thing. I hear it squeak when you rub it. I simply perceive it that way, and the best I can do is describe to you verbally what it’s like for me. Should you doubt that your friend is experiencing the balloon as sexy? Surely not. Why would they lie about that? You should believe, My friend is experiencing this balloon as sexy.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Now compare that to this case: Suppose you and I go hiking. We arrive a magnificent natural vista and spend a few minutes in quiet contemplation. At one point, you turn to me and say, “I think I’m having a religious experience. It’s like… it’s like I can feel God’s presence her with us, surrounding us. I feel her love and comfort and peace.” “I can’t point to any particular part of this vista as the religious part; I can’t show you how this scene is religious. I simply perceive it that way, and the best I can do is describe to you verbally what it’s like for me.” Should you doubt that your friend’s experience includes a component that seems religious? Hick wants to say that, as in the balloon case, it doesn’t really make a lot of sense to doubt them. Why would they lie about that? You should believe, My friend is experiencing this scene as religious. They are having that experience. But is that experience evidence? Again, Hick wants to say yes . But only for that person. These experiences should be counted as a kind of private evidence – not really any different in kind than the feeling of a headache. It’s true that your friend can’t share their religious experience with you. But, then, neither can they share the aching of their head.


SLIDE 31
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Why think that religious experiences are private evidence ? Because they seem like genuine experiences for the experiencer – just ones that can’t be shared with others.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
So here’s what Hick wants to say. In the same way that the aching of my head feels real to me – even though I can’t directly prove to other people that my head aches – my mystical experiences of God may feel real to me – even though I can’t directly prove to other people that I’m really having a religious experience. And, just as I wouldn’t tell my friend, “It’s irrational for you to believe you have a genuine headache right now b/c your “evidence” isn’t publicly available bro” Hick wants to say, we shouldn’t tell our religious friend, “It’s irrational for you to believe you’re having a genuine religious experience b /c your “evidence” isn’t publicly available ”

SLIDE 43
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Maybe the lesson we should draw is this, Theism seems like a rational response to profound religious experiences. Because such experiences seem to count as private evidence . BUT… It must equally be true that agnostics (and atheists!) without religious experiences are responding rationally to the public evidence . (c.f. The Problem of Divine Hiddenness )

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Why atheists? Why does this story change what we say about atheists? The atheist might rationally reason this way, “If God exists, She would want me to know that She exists and she would have the power to let me know! This is just the Problem of Divine Hiddenness. For me, then, the fact that I haven’t had any religious experiences seems itself to be some evidence against the existence of god. (That is, the fact that there are no religious experiences in my life is some private evidence that I have have that there are some unnecessary absences in the world. Which means, I have some private evidence that the Problem of Divine Hiddenness argument is sound. So I’m rationally justified in rejecting the 3-O God.)”
