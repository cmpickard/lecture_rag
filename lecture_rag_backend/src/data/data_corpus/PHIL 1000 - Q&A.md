
## **Quick Def of evidence**
**Lecture:** The Problem of Induction

"Evidence" is being used here to mean something like, "an observation that seems to count in favor of (or justify) a particular belief." Public evidence consists in observations that we can share (e.g. photos, videos, arguments.) Private evidence consists in observations that we can't share. (e.g. the feeling of my head aching \-- i can \*tell\* you about it, but i can't share the feeling.)

Why should we think there's such a thing as private evidence? The basic thought is: sometimes we really do experience things that genuinely seem to justify our having certain beliefs (e.g. an experience of seeing an angel descend from the heavens to speak to me, a headache), even though we cannot share those experiences with others. The fact that we cannot share those observations doesn't seem to render them *evidentially worthless*, in other words.

## **On the connection between the evidence principle and the problem of induction (and why philosophers mostly act as though the Problem can be solved)**
**Lecture:** The Problem of Induction

You've hit on an important problem / methodological point.

The Fine-tuning Argument relies on this Evidence Principle and, you might well wonder, what is the justification for that principle? How does it relate to the Problem of Induction?

The answer is: the Evidence Principle is inductively justified and it assumes that the Problem of Induction can be resolved.

 

To the first point:

The evidence principle relies on some strong assumptions about the uniformity of nature ("in the past, evidence has been like this, so all evidence must be like that...") and it is, itself, largely plausible b/c of it's own history of successes. (e.g. "the evidence principle has worked so far in that past, so...") I think the evidence principle is a good, plausible principle\! We should accept it. But doing so requires us to set aside the Problem of Induction.

This is an important methodological point about philosophy that I cannot stress enough:

Basically, all philosophers go about their philosopher-business on the assumption that the Problem of Induction fails. Not because we all agree about why it fails. (And, indeed, for many us, we actually don't have any solution in mind\!) But we assume it fails because we can't really do otherwise. It's paralyzing to hold onto Inductive Skepticism; your life comes to a grinding halt. So we don't do that\! We are aware of the Problem, but must operate under the assumption that it's soluble. (likewise, we're all aware of Descartes' arguments for external world skepticism\! But we continue to operate as though we can know things about the external world.)

The Evidence Principle seems really good from that assumption. It's a genuinely attractive principle, so long as we assume that inductive reasoning is justified.

## **On circular reasoning and why we can’t conclude \~C from a bad argument for C**
**Lecture:** Intro to Arguments

Maybe this will help you get your head around circular reasoning?

 

Consider this bad argument:

P1) Everything is blue

P2) The sky is a thing

C) The sky is blue

 

Notice, this argument sucks. (P1 is false\!) But the conclusion is true. You should believe that the sky is blue, but you shouldn't believe that on the basis of this argument.

In general, one should be careful to separate these two thoughts: (i) This is a bad argument for C, (ii) C is false. We should not infer from a bad argument that the conclusion of that argument is false. We should only infer that this particular argument fails.

Something similar can be said of circular reasoning. I can give you a circular justification for a true claim:

You should believe that the sky is blue because I say so. You should believe whatever I say because I'm infallible. And you should believe I am infallible because I am telling you that I am. And since I'm infallible, you can believe it. Etc..

Once again, the sky really is blue. But you shouldn't believe that merely because of the last paragraph of (bad, circular) argumentation.

SO. All that's to say: if you know X to be true, you shouldn't doubt X merely because someone gives you a circular justification for X. We can give bad justifications for true claims\!  
But the trouble arises when you only have circular justifications for X. If a circular justification is all you have, is your only reason for believing X, you're in trouble.

## **Why the Prob of Ind implicated probabilistic reasoning too**
**Lecture:** The Problem of Induction

This is good\! And it's an important extension of the Problem of Induction.

 

As it happens, our use of probability itself seems to be reliant on inductive reasoning.

 

Think about, e.g., one of those exercises you do in Middle School where you flip a coin 100 times and record all the outcomes. The teacher (and the students) all go into the exercise believing that they know what will happen, roughly speaking. Their beliefs are probabilistically justified. And that justification turns on (a) things they know about probability and (b) past experiences.

But both (a) and (b) are themselves inductive.

One thing you might know about probability is that coin flips are independent events. But that's an inductive bit of knowledge. (Do you see why? I'll leave you to fill in the details. But think about how we learn of causal regularities.)

One thing you know about past experiences is that these exercises consistently produce the same sorts of results. That's also an inductive bit of knowledge. (Again, I'll leave you to fill in the details.)

 

Or think about a bookie who has set odds on 5,000-ish sports events over any given week. Let's suppose this week that 100 of those events have 9 to 1 odds. (90% chance of one outcome; 10 % chance of the other.) He gambles his livelihood on those events panning out in a particular way. He expects, roughly 90 of those events will go the way I expect and the other 10-ish will be surprise upsets. That expectation is inductive. (It's worked so far\! He's making a living, after all.)

And, we might note: Where do his odds-assignments come from in the first place? How does he decide how to arrange each bet? From the past \- from past observations of events like these. That, too, is inductive.

So, canonically at least, probabilistic reasoning has been thought to be hostage to the Problem of Induction, too. (In my logic courses \-- and in logic textbooks more generally \-- probabilistic arguments are taught as a kind of inductive argument, for precisely this reason.)

## **On solipsism**
**Lecture:** Skepticism

RE: Solipism

Yeah, that's a good question, too. *Solipsism* is the actually the name for a few different views.

The strongest version says: *Only* *I exist; there are no other beings with minds in the universe.*

The interaction between external world skepticism and that kind of solipsism is kinda complicated.

First, external world skepticism is the view that *we can't **know** whether the external world is real.* Thus, the external world skeptic is not a (strong) solipsist. At most, she will say that **she doesn't know** whether or not she's the only person in the universe.

Second, most of the skeptical hypotheses we considered actually don't entail solipsism\! Like, suppose you are being tricked by an evil demon. If so, there are actually 2 people in the universe: you and the demon. Hence, solipsism is false.  Similarly, suppose you are being tricked by a bunch of superneuroscientists. If so, there must be a few people in the universe: you and a bunch of scientists.

That said, there are other skeptical scenarios that would entail solipsism. Like, maybe you're a brain in a vat but the supercomputer that's running the simulation isn't conscious and there are no other conscious beings in that universe \-- just a big computer and a single brain. 

That scenario *does* seem to entail solipsism\! But, again, the external world skeptic isn't *confident* that she's a brain in a vat. So she'll never be confident that solipsism is true.

There are weaker forms of solipsism, too. And those *are* entailed by external world skepticism. 

One weaker version says: *One cannot know whether other minds exists.*

That version obviously does follow from external world skepticism. If I can't know whether *anything* outside of my own head exists, then I can't know whether *minds* outside my own head exist.

Does that answer your question?

## **For someone confused about skepticism, broadly**
**Lecture:** Skepticism

Great \- that's a really astute question\!

 

The grossly simplified version goes like this,

*The X Skeptic is someone who doesn't think we can have knowledge about X-things. So a **religion** skeptic doesn't think we can know things about God or the supernatural, including whether God actually exists. A **history** skeptic doesn't think we can know anything about the past. A **horse** skeptic doesn't think we can know anything about horses (including whether they even exist.)*

Obviously, some forms of skepticism are more plausible than others. No one is a horse skeptic; that's a silly position. But there are some religious skeptics\!

An **external world skeptic** is, thus, a person who doesn't think we can know things about the external world, including whether there even is such a thing.

Here's the more sophisticated explanation:

*Most people agree that **knowledge** requires **justification**. Suppose I just random pick a day (call it 'Z') from the calendar and then I form the belief that that's your birthday. And, let's suppose, I accidentally guess correctly. Do I **know** that Z is your birthday? Well, if you tell me "Hey\! You guessed correctly\!" then yes. I know it. But if you **don't** tell me, then surely not. Surely I don't **know** it\! I believe it, and my belief is accidentally true, but it's not knowledge.*

*But what is justification?*

*It's evidence, basically. Or, if you like, reasons. When I ask, "What's your justification for thinking that the earth is round?" I'm asking, "What evidence do you have?" or "What reasons led you to this belief?"*

*Justification comes from a bunch of different places. Our senses give us justification. The fact that **you saw** a buffalo on the grass, is a good reason to think there's a buffalo on the grass. Other people's testimony can give us justification. The fact that I **told you** that my favorite fruit is dates is a good reason to think that my favorite fruit is dates. Our memory can give us justification. Logic and reasoning can give us justification.*

*Here's why all that matters:*

*The External World Skeptic thinks that we cannot have knowledge **about some particular thing.** (that's what I was explaining above.) The External World Skeptic thinks that **no sources of justification can give us evidence about the external world.** A **religious skeptic** has a similar view about religious knowledge: no sources of justification can give us evidence about religion.*

*The Inductive Skeptic isn't like that. She doesn't think that we can have knowledge **that's justified using induction** (which is a form of reasoning.) The Inductive Skeptic thinks that **this particular source of justification (induction) is worthless and never gives us knowledge.** (Compare: a Memory Skeptic is someone who thinks that **memory is worthless and never gives us knowledge.** A **Testimony Skeptic** is someone who thinks that **other people's say-so is worthless and never gives us knowledge.**)*

 

If I had to guess, anyway, that's the source of the confusion: those two kinds of skeptics are *skeptical about very different sorts of things*. The External World Skeptic is skeptical that we can know **about** the external world. The Inductive Skeptic is skeptical that we can know **using** our inductive reasoning. 

Does that help?

##  **On the “no new evidence” argument for Steadfastism**
**Lecture:** Disagreement

Yeah, that's a nice suggestion\! That sounds like a common Steadfaster treatment of disagreement. (Which is to say: you're in good company\!)

Steadfasters often want to say:

*When you learn that an epistemic peer disagrees with you about X, you don't **at that moment** get any new evidence about X. (After all, we can pretty much guarantee that for every one of your beliefs, **someone** disagrees with you about it. So you don't really learn anything new when you locate that someone.) Since you don't get any new evidence, it would be irrational to change your confidence in X. **(Plausible Epistemic Principle:** one should only ever change one's beliefs or degrees of confidence in light of new evidence.)*

*That said, when you learn that an epistemic peer disagrees with you, you **do** now have an opportunity to ask them why. And it could be that their explanation **does** give you new evidence \-- some argument or consideration or reason you've not considered before. If that happens, then of course you should change your degree of confidence. But not otherwise.*

 

I think that's a pretty powerful argument for the Steadfast position\!

## **On the diff between the post-truth thesis and the echo chamber thesis**
**Lecture:** Echo Chambers and Epistemic Bubbles

According to the Post-truth Thesis, many people have stopped caring about reasoning / about truth / about rationality all together. For these people, the decision about whether to believe something is fundamentally *emotional* rather than *rational.*

 

According to Nguyen's preferred explanation (what we might call 'the Echo Chamber Thesis'), people still care about reasoning / about truth / about rationality. What's going on is that some people are *radically* mistaken about how evidence should be assessed. Such people *really do* care about truth and rationality, they are just getting things wrong because they're starting from false assumptions.

Here's a concrete case \- maybe that will help.

Suppose Trevor is really into some new diet craze \-- maybe the Beans Only diet \-- and is a member of a bunch of online communities for Beans Only eaters like him. Suppose some new scientific study comes out that says, with some force, that the Beans Only diet is very unhealthy. How will Trevor respond to this new information? Assuming he's going to react *badly* (irrationally), the Post-truth Thesis and the Echo Chamber Thesis actually make different predictions:

(i) The Post-truth Thesis expects Trevor to *not care* about this new research. Trevor *feels* that the Beans Only diet is healthy, and that's enough. He doesn't need to see "evidence" or "arguments". It doesn't matter what "rationality" demands. That stuff doesn't matter. He's found his truth, and his truth is the Beans Only diet.

(ii) The Echo Chamber Thesis expects Trevor to *challenge* this new research. Trevor has been told that nutritionists are in the pocket of Big Agriculture and that their research is fake and spurious. He looks through the new article, and, it seems to him, it's more of the same. Bad science paid-for by industry hacks. So, **he reasons,** he should dismiss this evidence. The **reasonable thing to do**, he concludes, is to continue believing that the Beans Only diet is good.

(There's a third option for Trevor, of course. He might simply *reason correctly*:

(iii) Ideally, if Trevor is being a responsible and rational agent, he will carefully consider the new study and, in light of this new evidence from experts, he will lower his confidence that Beans Only diet is a good idea.)

The main difference between the two Theses is this:

The Post-Truth thesis says people have **given up on reasoning.** The Echo Chamber thesis says people **are still reasoning but doing it badly.**

**For persistent confusions about Anderson’s argument**

Anderson is responding to an important argument from the Legalize / Normalize crowd. 

One of the premises of that argument is, *Sex work is in principle no different from other kinds of personal services.* Call this claim 'S'.

Anderson thinks that claim is false. **More precisely,** he thinks that *you* (or, *most people*) not actually believe S to be true. His argument is intended to pump your intuitions that S is false.

To do that he asks us to imagine a hypothetical world where sex work is legalized and where sex work is treated exactly the same as other personal services \-- is subject to the same regulations, laws, and norms. I went through 7 of those in lecture, trying to explain what such a world would be like. 

Anderson argues: If I really believed that S were true \-- that sex work were no different from other personal services \-- I would not be bothered by that hypothetical world. But most people *are* bothered by that hypothetical world. Thus, most people do not actually think S is true.

The mistake that lots of people are making is in thinking that Anderson is **making a prediction** about what would happen in our world if we legalized sex work. 

It worth re-emphasizing: ***He is not**; Anderson **is not making predictions** about what would happen if we legalized sex work. That is not the point.*

Maybe none of his hypotheticals would ever actually happen. Maybe the hypothetical world he imagines *could never* be our actual world.  That's fine. That fact (if it is a fact) is not a problem for his argument. All he needs for his argument to work is for most people to **dislike** his hypothetical worlds. His point is simply this, "Most people do not actually, intuitively judge that sex work is just like other personal services. Most people intuitively want to treat sex work differently; they **dislike** a world where it is treated exactly the same."

Thus, pointing out that Anderson's world would never happen *does not undermine his argument. (*Since this is straightforwardly a question about what Anderson's argument *is,* this is the sort of thing I might ask about on the exam\! \*waggles eyebrows suggestively\*)

To be clear, there *are* plenty of good reasons why we might be skeptical of Anderson's arguments\! I don't mean to suggest Anderson is *correct* about the Legalize/Normalize argument. The point is that "his hypothetical world is implausible" is not among those reasons. 

## **On one person’s modus tollens being another person’s modus ponens**
**Lecture:** Intro to Arguments

If I'm following your line of thought, you've suggested the following conditional is true,

If a person's valid consent is sufficient for waiving their right to bodily integrity, then assisted suicide (w/ consent) must be permissible (and, perhaps, should be legal, too.)

 

I think lots of people will agree with that conditional claim\! That is, lots of people will probably agree that the permissibility of assisted suicide (with consent) really does follow, if we accept that that valid consent is sufficient waiving the right to bodily integrity.

 

Here's an interesting Logic Fact\! 

This conditional actually gives rise to two different arguments, depending on whether you think the antecedent (i.e. the IF... bit) is obviously true or the consequent (i.e. the THEN... bit) is obviously false.

Arg \#1:

P1) If consent is sufficient for waiving my right to bodily integrity, then assisted suicide (w/ consent) must be permissible.

P2\*) Consent clearly is sufficient for waiving my right to bodily integrity

C\*) Assisted suicide must be permissible 

 

Arg \#2

P1) If consent is sufficient for waiving my right to bodily integrity, then assisted suicide (w/ consent) must be permissible.

P2^) Assisted suicide (even w/ consent) is clearly NOT permissible

C^) Consent is NOT sufficient for waving my right to bodily integrity

 

Those are both valid arguments. And both appeal to the truth of P1. The question is simply this: 

is P2\* true or is P2^ true? Pick one\! 

You seem to endorse Arg \#2\! Presumably, because you feel confident that P2^ is true. 

However, given public opinion polling on this issue (see here (Links to an external site.) for instance), I suspect many other people will endorse Arg \#1 instead\! They feel confident that P2\* is true. 

 This is true of all conditionals. In principle, we may agree that a particular conditional is true, but still arrive at very different conclusions because we disagree about the truth value of the parts of the conditional. (One of us thinks the antecedent is obviously true; from which it follows that the consequent is true. The other of us thinks the consequent is obviously false; from which follows that the antecedent is false.)

## **Another counterexample to the H.O.P. (minimum wage)**
**Lecture:** On Organ Sales

Yeah, that seems plausible to me, too. I think that *if* the U.S. were to legalize kidney sales, then we'd be likely to enact protections to prevent the sorts of *debt collateral* concerns that Satz is raising.

But \-- here's the interesting bit \-- supposing that's true, that doesn't mean Satz is wrong about the HOP. The HOP, recall, is a really strong claim:

**HOP \--** *It never does or could make someone worse off to give them one more choice.*    \<= (that's supposed to apply always in every possible case\!)

Satz' example still seems sufficient for showing that that claim is false \-- adding the option to selling a kidney could make someone worse off, if kidneys were then considered debt collateral. 

Here's a more interesting counter-example that Satz briefly mentions in the article, but develops more fully elsewhere:

Satz suggests that minimum wage laws might also count as a counterexample to the Harmless Options Principle.

A person who accepts the H.O.P. might reason like this: Suppose we abolished the minimum wage laws. People could now work for 1 dollar an hour. And this is harmless\! We've just given people one more option than they had before\! As always, having one extra option is not bad for you. If you don't want to work for 1 dollar an hour, just ignore your new option.

Satz worries that this new option could, in principle, affect the decision-making of other agents, elsewhere in the economy and end up being a net-harm for people. She's particularly concerned about effects on the public school system.

In one sense, the whole point of the public school system, from the perspective of the Federal Govt, is to educate and train citizens well enough for them to become contributing members of society. So understood, the minimum wage laws actually represent a meaningful constraint on the public education system: Since no citizen can legally work for less than $7.50, we must educate our citizens at least well enough for them to be able to produce $7.50 worth of value / hour. If we fail at that, our citizens will be unemployable and our school system will have failed in its single, overriding aim.

If the minimum wage laws were abolished, Satz thinks, this constraint would no longer be in place. In principle, if people could work for $2 / hr, then the state could get away with educating people only far enough for them to hit that level of marginal productivity. Put differently, Satz suggests that minimum wage laws are a kind of commitment that the state is making to educational standards. The state is effectively saying, "We regard it as unacceptable for our citizens to have a level of education below this threshold – the $7.25 / hour level of marginal productivity – and we are committed to ensuring that all citizens are skilled enough and educated enough to qualify for jobs paying above that minimum wage." That commitment undergirds the education system and has an influence on all sorts of educational policy-making\!

When we abolish the minimum wage, we are just giving people more choices than they had before. (“Now you have access to lower-paying jobs\! You couldn't choose those before, but now you can\!") But, Satz says, that isn’t by definition harmless. There could be harmful effects where other agents change their behavior, in light of the knowledge that people have this new option. (in this case: the other agents she has in mind are the people in charge of public schooling.)

 

Of course, even if we decide that the HOP is false, we should still ask, Is there some weaker version of the HOP that avoids these counter-examples and that we should accept? Like, maybe: *In general, giving X one more option does not limit X’s freedom nor make X worse off than she was before \-- unless the addition of that option undermines other choices.* ?

That weaker principle may be enough to defend the legalization of kidney sales, so long as we put in place the kinds of protections you mentioned. (Where the point of those protections is to make sure that the extra choice to sell a kidney do not undermine any other choices.

## **On whether our work-is-virtuous intuitions are unreliable**

But let me pitch a different argument in support of Dahaner:

A lot of people have the intuition that people who get up early in the morning are more virtuous than people who get up late. (I also have this intuition.) This is a pretty old cultural belief, one that seems to date back, again, at least to the Puritans.

## **On Dahaner’s assumption that the ETU is possible**
**Lecture:** Technological Unemployment

Yeah, that's interesting\! As you point out: Dahaner's whole project is premised on this major assumption: we could eventually find ourselves in a world where poverty has been eliminated and the govt adequately provides for everyone.  
And... wow. That's a big assumption. And I think you're right to wonder about it. (Is that plausible? Could we ever arrive at that world? Could there be such a trustworthy and reliable govt?)  
   
I think it's worth asking: Is there any reason to care about Dahaner's project if we don't think the ETU could ever happen? 

And the answer, I think, is *probably yes*\!

There are a couple things that I think Dahaner might want to say here:

First, he might argue that the ETU is a future worth considering simply because... people are notoriously really awful at correctly predicting the future \-- especially the future course of technology. Like, *really* terrible. So when it comes to trying to imagine the future 100 or 200 years out from now, maybe all we can do is shrug and ask "What if?" questions. (c.f. *the entire genre of science fiction*). Some of those "What If"s may come to pass; most won't. But given how awful we are at predicting which are which, we can't afford to be picky about which futures we allow ourselves to contemplate.

Second, sometimes those "What if?" questions may tell us interesting and useful things about \*ourselves right now\*. Like, *Fahrenheit 451* is a big What If? book. But it's a worthwhile exercise in speculation because it tells us something interesting and important about the dangers of censorship, the value of education and literature, and the risks of authoritarianism. The fact that *Fahrenheit 451* will never happen is not a reason to stop reading or thinking about it\! It has value outside of it's predictive accuracy.

I suspect Dahaner would want to say something similar about the "What If?" exercise that he's engaged in. The fact that the ETU will never happen (if that is a fact\!) is not a reason to stop thinking about it\! It has value outside of its predictive accuracy. He might say,

*The Era of Technological Employment is a useful thought-experiment, even if it's not something we could ever realize in the real world, because it allows us to think about **the value of working** in isolation from **the value of having an income**. It gives us a helpful way to think about **why** we work and whether work is something worth doing for its own sake. If we find ourselves dreading the ETU, that strongly suggests that we think work is intrinsically beneficial. If we find ourselves yearning for the ETU, that strongly suggests that we think work is only beneficial insofar as it helps us materially provide for ourselves. And, either way, the reasons we have for liking/disliking the ETU will help us make sense of our feelings about work. That is, this What If? can be helpful even if it never comes to be.*

(Though, Dahaner would surely also grant: his project is more interesting and relevant if we think the ETU is a plausible future outcome\!)

## **On Bostrum’s thesis being wishy-washy**
**Lecture:** The Vulnerable World Hypothesis

I think he's basically trying his best to be honest with us about what he's doing: **he's trying to predict the future** and **that's a hard thing to do.** 

Especially when it comes to *technological* predictions, people are notoriously bad at guessing what the future will be like. Bostrum is sensitive to that problem, I think, and that's why he states his thesis weakly. He says, *I don't know if the Vulnerable World Hypothesis is true, but I don't think it's obviously false.*

Part of the problem is, as you suggest, that it's not even clear what solid proof would look like for this kind of future-prediction claim\! How could you ever possibly **prove** that the future will be such-and-such a way? So he doesn't try. He just wants us to think that it's **sensible** to be worried about humanity inventing a black ball technology. 

He could be mistaken about that\! Maybe the VWH **is** obviously false?

Or, he could be mistaken in not trying to prove the truth of the VWH\! Maybe he gave up to easily and should have looked for a better argument?
