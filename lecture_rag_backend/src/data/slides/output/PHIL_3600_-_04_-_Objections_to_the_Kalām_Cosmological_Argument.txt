[SLIDE BODY]
The Big Bang is when physical evidence started accumulating

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Hopefully, you can see the relevance for the Big Bang. Our best physics tells us that all of our data points back to the Big Bang. That’s the point in time when our current body of evidence started accumulating. But the fact that our evidence allows us to peer back to a time, T-nought, just shows that evidence started accumulating at T-0. It doesn’t tell us why that’s the date when evidence started accumulating. Maybe the Big Bang really is the start of the universe! That’s very possible! But it’s not the only possibility. For all we know, the Big Bang was just the point at which the old-version of the universe ended and ours began – perhaps because the universe regularly expands and contracts like a lung as some physicists have suggested. This seems like very serious error in the Cosmological Argument. On it’s face, at least, it simply seems false to claim that our best physics tells us that our universe began with the Big Bang. That’s only one possible explanation. (All of this also applies, mutatis mutandis, to the entropy-argument. It’s true that our current closed system began to increase in entropy at the Big Bang. But that’s not decisive evidence that the Big Bang was the first moment of the universe. Again, maybe the universe expands and contracts like a lung. Every contraction is followed by a Big Bang that starts the process over again, restarting the clock on entropy)

The thought has to be that the Hotel is impossible because it is actually infinite . But… is that right?

[PRESENTER NOTES]
That suggests another way to fill in the hole in Craig’s argument.… But we haven’t actually been given any reasons for thinking that’s true. And, if you think about it, there do seem to be other explanations that might be offered. Maybe the Hotel is impossible b/c the Hotel staff completes an infinite task in a finite amount of time. That could potentially explain all the absurdities! Of course you can’t complete an infinite task in hour – but that’s what happens in Hotel! So maybe what we learn from Hilbert’s hotel is that there cannot be any completed infinite tasks. (But, if so, that doesn’t prove that the universe has a beginning.) Or Maybe it’s that the Hotel is materially infinite. That is, the Hotel is infinitely in it’s material size, requiring an infinite amount of matter. (Indeed, simply filling the hotel full of people also requires an infinite amount of matter.) So maybe what we learn from the Hotel is that there cannot be any that is actual infinite in its material size. (Which – is plausible! The universe contains only finite materials.) But, again, if that’s the lesson, then we haven’t proven that the universe had a beginning.

All of that’s to say: We still need to know what, exactly, makes the Hotel impossible. That has not been clearly established. Only when we have that explanation in hand can we assess whether an infinitely old universe is also impossible for the same reason.

Lastly, take another look at the claim, Actual infinities are impossible . If this is required for the Cosmological Argument, the C.A. seems to undermine the doctrine that God grants eternal life . We all must eventually perish because the alternative – a life with no ending – is, we’re told, quite impossible.

[PRESENTER NOTES]

That is, if the Cosmological Argument works, it seems that the best God can do for us is give us a very long life that is ultimately finite. ... Thus, if the theist really want to insist on this point, on the impossibility of actually infinite things, then they must also be prepared to give up on parts of their faith. (Of course, that won’t be a problem for those who believe in a mere Creator God, but that’s… not very many people. Most theists believe in a personal, loving God and also believe in the promise of eternal life with that God in heaven.) That doctrine apparently cannot be sustained if one rests one’s faith on the Cosmological Arg

Lastly, we might worry that the Cosmological Argument commits the informal Fallacy of Composition . The Fallacy of Composition involves an inference of the form, Since all of X’s parts have the property P, the whole of X must also have P.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
This is an informal fallacy because it is often not the case that wholes have the same properties as their parts. Sometimes the whole’s properties are summations of their parts properties. Othertimes there are properties that emerge as the level of the whole but that are absent from the parts.

[SLIDE BODY]
A word of caution The Fallacy of Composition is – like all informal fallacies – a helpful rule-of-thumb . Not every argument that employs this pattern of reasoning is defective. (e.g. “All of X’s parts are made of wood. So X is made of wood”)

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Though, it’s worth noting, as with any informal fallacy it’s simply FALSE that every instance of this argument pattern is a mistake. For some properties and some wholes, wholes necessarily DO have the same properties as their parts. (e.g. “All of X’s parts are made of wood. So X is made of wood”) That’s a good inference. Don’t let some intro logic students talk you out of that argument merely because it has the same form as the Fallacy of Composition. Rookie mistake : Lots of intro logic students get so caught up pointing out informal fallacies that they forget that informal fallacies are crude rules-of-thumb. There’s even a name for this mistake: it’s called the Fallacy Fallacy. It’s the error of dismissing a good argument merely b/c it has the same form as an informal fallacy. (All of this is worth keeping in mind if you ever find yourself arguing on the internet. Internet people LOVE pointing out informal fallacies, but they’re really dumb about it.) ANYWAY: merely pointing out that the Cosmological Argument exhibits this same pattern of reasoning isn’t by itself sufficient for showing that there’s a reasoning error. What’s needed is some reason for thinking that the property in question (being caused) is something that the parts might have without guaranteeing that the whole will have it. I’m not sure what to say about that. At the very least, though, this line of reasoning may succeed at passing the burden to proof to the proponent of the Cosmological Argument: they need to explain why their argument doesn’t count as fallacious.
