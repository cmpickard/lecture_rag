[PRESENTER NOTES]
Recall once more, the shape of the discussion. We have this argument, The Problem of Evil, which observes an incompatibility between the world as it actually is and the world as it should be if the 3-O God exists. Most theists want to respond to this problem by arguing that all of the suffering in the world is necessary – all of it is needed to prevent some greater evil or to secure some greater good.


SLIDE 4
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
The Free Will Theodicy It is consistently worse to violate a human’s free will than to allow evil. Thus, the suffering that stems from human choices is necessary suffering.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Last time we looked at the FreeWill Theodicy. It says, roughly, that God knows that human freedom is more valuable than stopping evil. So She lets evils occur whenever stopping evil would require violating someone’s free will. We also looked at some objections to this Theodicy and tried to keep track of all the commitments the theist must make to hold onto her view.

The Soul-making Theodicy

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Today we’ll talk about a different theodicy. This one attempts to explain the necessity of suffering not by pointing out what we’d lose through God’s intervention but by emphasizing what we gain by dealing with evil ourselves.

[SLIDE BODY]
Motivation #1: God prefers human intervention to divine intervention.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
I want to offer two different motivations for the soul-making theodicy. The first one picks up on a thread from our conversation last time.


SLIDE 7
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
God could step in to help. But in Her moral perfection, She knows She shouldn’t. You could step in, too. Although far from morally perfect, you know that you should. (and God presumably wants you to!) How do we explain this strange asymmetry?

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Suppose you live next to a young couple with a baby. As best as you can tell, the couple is gone for 2-3 days at a time, leaving the baby alone at home. You hear it crying through your shared walls. You worry that it’s being severely underfed and under-nurtured. You think about calling child protective services, but you ultimately decide not to.


SLIDE 8
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
The Free Will Theodicy cannot explain this. For both you and God, helping means violating the free choice to ignore the baby. And if freedom is really more important than infant suffering, you must not help.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
This is what we talked about last time. This asymmetry is a very big problem for the Free Will Theodicy. To accept the Free Will Theodicy is to accept that human freedom is, like, the most valuable thing. But if that’s right, it’s hard to understand why we’d ever be morally required to violate the freedom of others in the name of stoppping evil.


SLIDE 9
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Maybe God wants to and does help? She uses you as her instrument of assistance. Maybe. But that would imply that God Herself is ready to step in if you refuse. That’s visibly not how these things go IRL. (If no human helps, the baby goes unhelped.)

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Here’s a different possible suggestion. The problem here is the implication that God is intent on helping. She is definitely going to make sure that the baby is helped. Which,simply, isnt’ true. That is quite clearly not the world we live in.


SLIDE 10
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Maybe it’s not really about the baby. Maybe it’s about you. Maybe God wants you to grow as a person . (And, frankly, you really need the practice.) So, yes, God could help the baby. But then what would you learn from that?

[PRESENTER NOTES]
But that suggests yet another possible story. We might think: God doesn’t want to be our safety net. We need to think of our world as our responsibility. If there’s some evil to be stopped, we need to see ourselves as the only ones to stop it. There’re no training wheels in this world.  If’ we’re to grow and mature, we have to struggle on our own.


SLIDE 11
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Soul-making Theodicy Suffering and struggle are necessary in that we need to endure them to secure the greater good of our own maturation, development, & communion with God .

[PRESENTER NOTES]
So this brings us to the Soul-making Theodicy... Here’s one way to motivate it We say: “All of the evils in the world are actually crises/opportunities. God is inviting us to step in and stop evil for ourselves. She is inviting us to become like her, to work together to make our world and ourselves better.” After all, the 3-O God is already morally perfect. But for you and I to become more like God, we need to practice. It’s like helping your child work on a math problem. It’s simply not very helpful if you do the problems for him. You could do them – you’re pretty good at math, look at you! – but you don’t need the practice. Your kid is the one who needs to practice; he needs to be the one who struggles.

The earth and human beings appear to be far more than 6,000 years old. The Garden of Eden story cannot really explain the necessity of suffering if it’s not true So here’s another path to the Soul Making Theodicy. Think about God as making humans through the process of evolution, as described by our best science. Turns out, when we do that, the Soul-making theodicy starts to look pretty good.

[SLIDE BODY]
Objection 1 : Many religions claim that people can be damned – i.e. sentenced to eternal posthumous punishment . Why would the 3-O God do that to her friends?

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Well… She wouldn’t. I think that’s what we have to say. Hell makes exactly zero sense, if you buy the soul-making theodicy. Two problems: (1) The soul-making God would never make a person, then spend a lifetime trying to improve them, and then throw up her hands when the going gets hard and the person resists getting better. She wouldn’t simply quit on someone in the midst of soul-making. (Nor, given her Omniscience and Omnipotence, is it possible for there to even BE a human who She can’t perfect with enough time and effort. God would know in advance exactly what to do to guide this person toward moral perfection and would have the power to make that happen.) (2) The soul-making God would never make a person, try to improve them, and then torture them forever for failing. That’s nonsense. God would know in advance that the person would fail. She created that person after all – and could have made them a little different if she was worried about failure. In her perfect moral character, it’s hard to imagine Her setting someone up for failure like that.


SLIDE 38
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Commitments of the Soul-making Theodicy There’s no damnation, and everyone ends up in heaven in the fullness of time.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
As we did last time, I want to make a list of all the commitments we’re forced to make in defending the Soul-making THeodicy. This is the first one. I personally don’t think this one is that big of a deal, but your mileage may vary. Some people may feel VERY CONFIDENT that there is a hell, and that individuals who go there are tortured forever. The soul-making theodicy is probably not for those people. I actually think, at that point, that the 3-O God is not for those people. If one believes that some humans are tortured infinitely by God for their finite Earthly crimes, then one believes in an afterlife that is plainly and fundamentally unjust. Thus, God is not omnibenevolent. =(

Objection 2 : Some people mature more than others. What does the 3-O God do with those who die without developing any significant virtue or spiritual maturity?

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Suppose the world ends tomorrow in a giant extinction event – a big meteor or something. Does God say, “Well, time’s up. Fuck it. Everyone gets an A”? Presumably not. The whole point is to perfect each human. If a person fails to be perfected during her time on earth, then God must continue her perfecting efforts after the person’s death. If you don’t get it done in time, you’re going to have to stay after class.

Objection 3 : What about people who die in childhood? Do they finish soul-making in heaven? If that’s an option, then why do some of us have to soul-make on the objectively shittier Earth?

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Before the advent of modern medicine, about ⅓ people died before the age of 5. Obviously, no 5 year old is a morally perfected being. Thus, we need some sort of story about how those individuals finish soul-making. We have two choices: They finish in heaven They finish elsewhere It’s hard to believe that they finish in heaven. If ⅓ of people die young and get to soul-make in heaven, then the world starts to look patently unfair. ( i want to soul-make in heaven, too! Why didn’t I get to die in childhood?!) It’s hard to imagine the 3-O having such an unfair plan. Which means, those who die in childhood must soul-make elsewhere. There’s only one place we know of that God has carefully designed for soul-making: the earth. Thus, those who die early prolly continue soul-making on earth… meaning, what exactly? One obvious option is reincarnation. Those who die early are sent-back into new bodies and allowed to continue soul-making.

[SLIDE BODY]
Objection 5 : The average life today is far less painful than the average life ~300 years ago. If suffering is the path to improvement, are we now soul-making slower? Do I do a bad thing by giving my child Advil? Is it better to just let her suffer/grow? Does the improvement of the species not slow the improvement of individuals? Like, if we as a species stop fighting wars, doesn’t that mean that future people will be deprived of all the great soul-making benefits of war?

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Here’re two of the more troubling implications of this observation. The soul-making theodicy seems to treat all suffering as an important growth opportunity. If a morally-perfect God sees suffering that way, then a morally-perfect human should too. But that means that a genuinely morally good person may sometimes correctly refuse to alleviate the suffering of others . She will say, “Yes your tooth aches, and I could give you a root canal right now, but you’ll grow more if we wait a week, so let’s do that. I don’t want to prolong the amount of time you spend on Earth after all!” Likewise, the improvement of the species seems at odds with the improvement of the individual. Individuals need to suffer to get better while societies need to prevent suffering to get better.
