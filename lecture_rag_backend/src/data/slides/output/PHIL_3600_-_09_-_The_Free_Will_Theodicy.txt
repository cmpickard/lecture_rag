[SLIDE BODY]
P1) If the 3-O God exists, there could never be even one instance of unnecessary suffering . (b/c She would know about it, want to prevent it, and be able to) P2) There has been at least one instance of unnecessary suffering . C) The 3-O God does not exist The Problem of Evil Possible Responses: Reject P1 Reject P2 Accept C Preferred theist option

[PRESENTER NOTES]
To my knowledge, almost no one has tried to argue that P1 is false. It just… looks very obviously correct, to both theists and atheists. How could an all-powerful, all-knowing, perfectly good being possibly allow unnecessary suffering? And of course, most theists aren’t going to like the third option. They want to continue believing in a 3-O God. Thus, we’re left with Option 2: we have to find a way to reject P2. We must argue, There has never been – not even once – an instance of unnecessary suffering.

It might seem like no-big-deal for God to step in. But consider for a moment what the world would look like if god always did that. Imagine a world in which God makes all of our choices for us. She picks out our outfits in the morning, decides what we’ll eat, where we’ll work, who we’ll live with, how much Netflix we’ll watch, how often we’ll sing God’s praises, etc. God decides whom we’ll love and with whom we’ll procreate – and then, of course, once our children are born, God takes full control of their lives too. Each life is, of course, morally perfect: You always do the right thing (b/c you have no choice.) You love your neighbor as yourself (b/c you have no choice.) You love God with all your heart, mind, soul, and strength (b/c you have no choice.) (think of this as the Puppetmaster world – where God is pulling all the strings)

[SLIDE BODY]
The Free Will Theodicy Freedom is good because it gives our choices weight, meaning, & power. It forces us to struggle and grow. Freedom is bad because we may misuse it to do evil. Thus, God faces a choice, preserve our freedom and allow evil OR prevent evil and revoke our freedom

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Clearly, then, human freedom has both pros and cons... And we can see this puts God in a bit of pickle… The Free Will Theodicy consists of the view that the first choice is allows better. It’s always better for God to preserve our freedom and allow evil than to prevent evil and revoke our freedom

[SLIDE BODY]
Unnecessary Evil Candidates War Disease (e.g. Tay-Sachs, Alzheimer's) Natural Disasters Animal suffering Genocide Sociopathy Torture

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Look again at our list of unnecessary evil candidates. A lot of them seem to stem from human choices – and so lots of them can be explained by the free will theodicy. BUT the free will theodicy doesn’t obviously explain all instances of suffering. It’s not obvious that we can explain Rowe’s fawn who burns to death in the forest or the badness of Alzheimer’s by pointing to the value of human freedom. When God is deciding whether to let someone get Alzheimer’s, it’s not clear that She is deciding whether to violate someone’s freedom. Surely God could prevent disease without literally STOPPING someone from making a choice – she could just wave her hand, and all the mis-folded proteins in the person’s brain would disappear, leaving that person none the wiser and also Alzheimer’s free. That sounds like a win-win.


SLIDE 18
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Can the Free Will Theodicy explain the necessity of natural evils? Swinburne: Yes . God allows, e.g., rabies and tornadoes so that we can learn of those things – and that learning itself enhances our freedom .

[PRESENTER NOTES]
That said, many proponents of the Free Will Theodicy insist that it does and can explain the necessity of natural evil. The Swinburne reading for this module, for instance, offers us a story about why natural evils are necessary parts of human freedom. His story is that God allows us to experience natural evils – things like tornadoes and rabies – so that we can acquire important knowledge about the natural world. By watching people die of rabies, we both acquire information about the disease itself AND acquire extra options in the future. In the future, we’ll be able to choose whether to intentionally infect people with rabies, knowing what that will do to them, or can choose to research a cure. Those extra options mean extra freedom. And since extra freedom is super super valuable, natural evils are necessary suffering.

If you’re still feeling shaky about that reply from Stump, try this: Imagine you have a friend who keeps throwing punches at your head. He hits you right in the jaw. You say, “OW. STOP. Why are you doing that?”And he says, “I’m trying to train you to dodge punches.” To which you reply, “Why? I don’t need to learn how to do that. I don’t ever get into fights.” Your friend says, “Yes, but in the future, I’m going to continue trying to punch you in the head, so I’m teaching you how to dodge NOW so that you can avoid my punches in the future.”Hopefully, you can see why that reply is unsatisfying and circular. Your friend has absolutely failed to justify his decision to punch you in the face. If he would just stop being an asshole and throwing punches at your face, your “bad at dodging” problem would resolve itself. Stump’s thought is: that’s what God sounds like, on Swinburne’s story. Why does God keep punching us in the head with rabies? How is that justified? How is that necessary? Swinburne says, WELL, in the future, God is going to continue trying to punch us in the head with rabies. In her perfect Omniscience, she’s knows that to be true. So she’s trying to teach us how to dodge those punches now so we can avoid those punches in the future. As before, that story is profoundly unsatisfying. If God would just stop being an asshole and giving us rabies, the “we don’t understand rabies” problem would resolve itself.

Here’s a second worry about the Free Will Theodicy. ... That’s…. Weird. If I’m choosing something out of habit, I’m not struggling with it. Thus, we can’t point to the good of the struggle when trying to explain why God allows habitual evil. But since God does allow people to be habitually evil, the Free Will Theodicy is going to have to affirm that those free choices are more valuable than the interests of the people who are harmed by habitual, unthinking acts of evil.
