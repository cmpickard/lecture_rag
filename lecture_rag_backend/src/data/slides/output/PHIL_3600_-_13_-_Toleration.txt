What is toleration?

[PRESENTER NOTES]
The easiest way to get at it, is probably by looking a few cases – and by contrasting civility with mere toleration. Civility involves communicating in a way that signals respect and consideration . Toleration involves, simply, taking no active steps to suppress or eradicate something you dislike.

So, what are some reasons to be tolerant , from a truth-neutral perspective? (Not looking for full-fledged arguments, here – just considerations in favor of tolerance.) Quiz

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Here by “truth-neutral”, I mean that we’re looking for reasons that everyone can appreciate – as opposed to reasons that are only moving if you have certain political or religious or philosophical beliefs. So, for example, “One reason to be tolerant is that the Pope says tolerance is good.” is not a truth-neutral reason. Non-Catholics mostly won’t care about that. On the other hand, basically everyone thinks killing is bad so it would count as a truth-neutral reason to point out that “Effective intolerance may sometimes require us to kill people.”  You don’t have to belong to any particular ideological group to care about that. So: what are some truth-neutral reasons to like tolerance – some reasons that we can mostly all agree on? Think about that for a second; try to come up with a few.


SLIDE 23
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
What is probably the best known defense of toleration comes from John Stuart Mill (1806 - 1873).

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Mill’s version is the one you all probably heard in your civics or government classes in high school. Mill wants to give us a Neutralist Tally of all the great benefits of toleration, a list that will be acceptable to all people, regardless of their religious creed or political party. Mill’s idea is basically this, Let me list off to you all the great things we get from being tolerant. And since I’m trying to argue on behalf of society at large, I’ll restrict my list to only those benefits that we can all appreciate and enjoy – nothing controversial! Christians and atheists alike can agree on this list; Republicans and Democrats both. Then he argues that that list, when added together, clearly shows that the cost of intolerance is too high – that it’s best for society if we tolerate bad ideas.

Another risk is that we might suppress a partially true/partially false view. Truth and error may be found combined in one package deal, so that there's no way of suppressing the error without also suppressing the truth So for example, if you suppress Marx’s books because you think communism is false and dangerous, you also risk suppressing the parts of Marx where he says other stuff that might be true and interesting and important


SLIDE 27
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Dead dogma

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Another reason to like tolerance is that it forces us to debate and argue over which views are true. And one important reason that debate and argument are good is that they help us develop and deepen our understanding of our own positions. By being challenged, we are forced to think a little more carefully about why we believe what we believe. So Mill worries: If we suppress all the bad views, future generations may have true beliefs but without actually understanding why those beliefs are true. Having never had to defend their positions, they will have dead dogmas – true beliefs that are poorly understood and held for shallow reasons. Since it’s important both to have true beliefs and to understand them, we should be wary of suppressing all opposition to our own views. Opposition is good for us!

SO recall our original question. ... Recall, this problem arose specifically because we were concerned about people who are exclusivists about salvation – i.e. people who think that their own religion is the only way to be saved.
