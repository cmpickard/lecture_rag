A theory that makes testable predictions and whose predictions consistently come true, is thereby better supported. The ability to make accurate predictions is a powerful form of evidence. The theory of general relativity, for instance, makes a bunch testable predictions about the universe. And it turns out, all of those predictions come true, are verified. Those verified predictions count in favor of the theory! Surely the same applies to any religion that makes testable predictions.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Given our previous discussion, it seem we can rule out the second option. If there WERE one particular religion that stands out as the most and only rational option, it would have to be the one that is best supported by the evidence. But s ince there doesn’t seem to be a particular religion that is better supported than the others, there is no one religion that can uniquely claim to be the only rational one. So we’re left with two options: We can either say that it’s always irrational to believe in a religion. Or, we can say that lots of religions can count as rational. That first option is, obviously, pretty unappealing to all theists, of every religion. So let’s set that aside for now. But what about the 3rd option? Can’t we just say that lots of religions are rational?Maybe! But it turns out, that view has some problems of its own.

“So religious [pluralism] poses a problem… If we retain our [own religious] beliefs because we judge that they are rational, and also judge that the conflicting beliefs of the atheist and of other religions are rational, we cannot maintain that the rationality of each belief makes it likely to be true…. [PRESENTER NOTES]
Here’s how Zagzebski puts it. Let me try to gloss the problem she has in mind here.


SLIDE 53
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
There’s supposed to be a tight connection between rationality and truth . To call a belief ‘rational’ is to approve of it as likely to be true , given the evidence .

[PRESENTER NOTES]
To see the concern, we need to say a bit about rationality claims. What does it mean to say that a belief is rational ? One obvious thing we’re doing when we call a belief ‘rational’ is approving of it and complimenting it – more particularly, we are complimenting it as truth-apt, as a good belief to have, given one’s interest in believing true things. That is to say... … Put differently, it’s a good thing to have rational beliefs! A person with all and only rational beliefs is doing better than a person with lots of irrational beliefs. And, more precisely, they are doing better truth-wise. The person with lots of irrational beliefs is, thereby, a person who surely has lots of false beliefs.


SLIDE 54
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
(no text on this slide)

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Here’s an easy example: Suppose you believe that it’s going to rain on Tues, Wed, or Thurs (and quite possibly all of them) after looking at this forecast. I’d call that a rational belief, given the evidence. Good job! Good beliefs. Now, of course, it certainly could turn out that it rains none of those days. Your rational beliefs could turn out to be false. But they’re still good beliefs. The alternative beliefs – that it won’t rain on Tues nor on Wed nor on Thurs – are clearly worse beliefs. Indeed, unless you have some special insight into the weather, those seem like positively irrational beliefs. They’re way more likely be false! (Again, they could turn out to be true beliefs! Irrational beliefs can sometimes be true. But they are still bad beliefs because they are unlikely to be true. If you looked at this forecast and concluded that it’s gonna be nonstop sunshine all week, you’d be doing a bad job at believing.In sum, the point is this: There is supposed to be a tight connection between rationality and truth. To call a belief rational is, usually, just is to say that it’s the belief that’s most likely to be true, given the evidence.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
So. This gives rise to a problem for the person who wants to say, most religions are raitonal. To get at the problem, let me offer an analogy. … The point being, this is a weird thing to insist on. Aaron thinks his belief is rational, even though there’s only a 10% chance that it’s true. And aaron also thinks that Rakim’s belief is rational, even though there’s only a 10% chance that his belief is true. Those two beliefs beliefs are incompatible and are probably false – and yet, he insists, are rational to have. In this case, it seems, it’s really not much of a compliment to call those beliefs rational. They aren’t, actually, likely to be true. They are very, very likely to be false.

Very very likely to be false, but rational. Good belief! I approve. Which, again, a strange use of the word ‘ rational’. That usage really unmoors the approval of the belief from the likelihood that the belief is true.
