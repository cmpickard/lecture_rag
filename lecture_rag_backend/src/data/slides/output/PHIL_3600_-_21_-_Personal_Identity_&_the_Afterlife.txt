“You could never step into the same river twice” – Heraclitus

[PRESENTER NOTES]
All of this so far is, I take it, common sense. But that’s not to say it’s uncontroversial. The Presocratic philosopher Heraclitus famously thought that NOTHING ever survived a change – a view he famously encapsulated with this quote. (Here the thought is: by the time you go to step into the river a second time, everything’s different: the banks have shifted a little, different waters swirl around your feet. And since everything’s different, it’s not really the same river.) To change, he thought, is necessarily to be destroyed. No changes are survivable.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
On the same body view, the persistence conditions for persons are like the persistence conditions for clubs.Imagine a sorority. Every year, 25% of its members graduate and a new class of roughly the same size enters. That is to say, year-to-year, the club is mostly the same, but some of its parts are swapped out for others. In the long run, of course, none of the members stays the same. But that doesn’t mean the sorority is destroyed every 4 years or so. Rather, we might say that the sorority persists through time because there is a continuity of membership. The members are swapped out slowly: as a few old members are lost, a few new members are brought in and integrated into the club alongside the remaining members. It’s the continuity that makes the sorority count as the same from one year to another. (Compare: suppose there was no continuity. Each year, all the old members are kicked out, and an entirely new group brought in. That seems like a situation in which the old sorority is destroyed and a new one created every year. No continuity of membership, so no survival.)This is what the Same Body view says about personal identity over time.Obviously, we know that human bodies are constantly undergoing change and replacement. Every minute of every day, old cells are dying and new cells are being born. Over the course of a decade, nearly all of your old atoms have been exchanged for new ones. But that doesn’t destroy you. You survive the change because there is a continuity of membership. As a few old parts are lost, a few new parts are brought in and integrated into the body alongside the remaining bits. It’s the continuity that makes the body count as the same from one year to another.

SLIDE 22
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
Person 1 Person 3 remembers being Person 2 remembers being Quiz

[PRESENTER NOTES]
One final wrinkle: Suppose Person 2 remembers being Person 1. And Person 3 remembers being Person 2. But Person 3 does not remember being Person 1.Quiz question: Why is this potentially a problem? It’s a problem b/c it seems to lead to a contradiction! We have to say: Person 3 is NOT the same as person 1 b/c 3 doesn’t remember being 1. But also, 3 IS the same as 1 b/c 3 = 2 and 2 = 1 and identity is a transitive relationship. So Person 3 both is and is not the same as person 1. That’s a bad result!How do we fix this problem?Well, sophisticated versions of the mental content view usually have a continuity condition, similar to the one used by the Same Body view. We say: the memory connection is also transitive. What matters isn’t whether 3 remembers 1 directly but whether there’s a chain of memory connecting 3 to 1. (if 3 remembers 2 and 2 remembers 1, 3 is the same as 1) So the view is usually stated like this: Person 3 is identical to anyone she remembers being AND anyone that any of those people remember being AND anyone that THOSE people remember being, and so it.

Physical things interact physically with other physical things – and we basically understand those interactions. That’s what the entire discipline of physics is all about. We must now try to imagine a brand-new sort of interaction, a kind unstudied by physicists: a non-physical interaction between matter located in space and time on the one hand and immaterial, unlocated, non-physical soul-stuff on the other. Since our best science only has physical instruments with which to record and measure physical interactions, we must anticipate that these non-physical interactions will be, in themselves, undetectable. [PRESENTER NOTES]
So here’s the problem (again, insofar as you share my intuitions about those cases). Our question is... Most people who like the same soul view DO actually believe that their soul includes all of their mental content – their memories, personality, beliefs, goals etc.That’s fine! That’s a coherent view. BUT the point is this: On that view, it seems kinda misleading to say that God needs to preserve your soul to bring you to heaven. She does, maybe, but the soul isn’t what matters. The is just the vessel for the important stuff. Think of it this way: on Option 2 – when the resulting person is really just Julius Caesar reincarnated – Julius Caesar’ soul is no more and no less the cup that holds his mind. When we put the cup and its contents into a new body, we thereby put Julius Caesar in a new body. But JC isn’t essentially the cup; that’s confused. He’s the content. It seems like we could imagine God pouring that content into a different cup. If She wanted, God could cut and paste your mental content from your current soul into a new soul. And, intuitively I think, that new soul would be you. Maybe that happens every night. Maybe human souls are weak and crumbly like Dixie cups, and so God has to copy our content into a new one every night when we sleep! That would be ok though! The important bits would still be saved; you would still be saved. We can tell, it seems, because you wake up each morning with the exact same memories and personality.

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Again, we can get at the problem with a case. Suppose you step into a Star Trek-style teleporter – a machine can move you about the universe at the speed of light. Here’s how it works: you step in the machine and press the TELEPORT button. The machine quickly scans your body, noting the exact location and arrangement of every one of your sub-atomic parts, and then transmits that information to another teleportation machine (on say Mars). The first machine then disassembles your body into its component atoms while the second machine perfectly re-assembles your body on Mars (using a different batch of atoms of course. Your old atoms are still on Earth) BOOM – you’re on Mars now. So far so good. That seems basically coherent – if technologically unfeasible at present. But now imagine that the transporter machine malfunctions. The copy is still created on Mars BUT the machine on Earth fails to disassemble the original copy. So now there are two humans running around with the exact same arrangements of atoms, the exact same memories, personalities, etc.


SLIDE 63
----------------------------------------
[SLIDE BODY]
God could create a duplicate-you right now , in heaven. But that person wouldn’t be you . After all, you’re you. But that means sharing memories and personality is insufficient for identity .

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Here’s the punchline. Now imagine God doing that very same thing to you right now in heaven… In other words, the mere fact that two persons have all the same memories and personality doesn’t make them literally one single person. Since it’s possible for God to create cognitive duplicates of you (beings who have your memories/personality but aren’t you) , there must be more to you than just your personal content. (And it won’t work to simply say, “But God would never do that! She would wait until I’m dead to create a copy in heaven.” That might be true, but it’s beside the point. The point is that it’s not enough to simply create a duplicate of you in heaven. Since the duplicate isn’t you in the case where you’re still alive on Earth, surely it’s not you in the case where you’ve just died on earth.


SLIDE 64

[SLIDE BODY]
O bjection Identity seems like a 1-to-1 relation: in the future, there will be either zero or exactly 1 person who is identical to you . But on the mental content view, there can be 2 or more people identical to you .

[PRESENTER NOTES]
Here’s the more general worry: The mental content view is concerning b/c it violates a core belief we have about identity: that each of us is, essentially, exactly one person. In future, either I won’t exist at all or there will be exactly one person who is me. The mental content view doesn’t abide by this principle. Since multiple future people could have my same mental content (could have my personality and remember being me) multiple future people could, on the mental content, view be me. That seems wrong!
